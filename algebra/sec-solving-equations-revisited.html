    Subsection Applications

    Before we get going too far, let’s just point out that the previous two sections didn’t mention applications. Those sections are instead foundational skills for mathematics we will see in this and future sections. It will happen from time to time that there are sections without applications, and instead the math there builds to other math we need. I hope you will see it as the intention of being honest with you in which sections have more direct applications and which sections only will have indirect applications.
    🔗
    This section reviews the math skills we’ve learned, using these skills after we discuss how to represent information from various contexts in the form of expressions and equations. We will be able to answer a variety of application questions:
    1. You need \(12,500\) miles for a flight. Your credit card usually transfers miles at a \(1:1\) ratio, but is currently offering a \(10\%\) bonus on all transfers. That is, when you transfer \(1,000\) miles to the airline, instead of getting the usual \(1,000\) miles, you will get \(1,100\) miles because of the \(10\%\) bonus. How many miles should you transfer to book the flight while taking advantage of the transfer bonus?
      🔗
      🔗
    2. You have a scholarship which allocates spending \(1500\) dollars on a laptop, including the tax. Assuming that tax is \(4\%\text{,}\) what is the pre-tax price of the laptop you can afford using this scholarship?
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Alice leaves from City X to head to City Y starting at noon traveling at \(50\) miles per hour. City X and City Y are \(200\) miles apart. Beth leaves from City Y to head to City X at \(30\) minutes past noon traveling at \(60\) miles per hour. How much time does Beth drive for?
      🔗
      🔗
    4. The CDC recommends hand sanitizer that’s at least \(60\%\) alcohol to deactivate the virus that causes COVID-19, but stores are out of stock due to overpurchasing. You have a \(32\) fl oz bottle of \(91\%\) isopropyl alcohol at home. It’s a good thing you bought this years ago, but since its strength is more than enough, you don’t want to waste it. How much lotion (with no alcohol in it) should you mix in to create a homemade hand sanitizer that’s \(60\%\) alcohol?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Expressions representing contextual quantities

    We start by looking at some expressions that involve percentages. First, we examine expressions involving percentages, and then our work will get brought up when we set up equations involving percentages in the next part of this section. Before we begin, there is an important principle about percentages to mention.
    🔗

    Principle 1.8.1.

    As we build expressions, whenever there is a percentage involved, ask and keep track: what are we taking a percentage of. We will end up multiplying the decimal version of a percentage by another quantity in the problem: the quantity that we multiply the decimal by is the direct answer to what we are taking a percentage of.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.8.2.

    A laptop has a price of \(\$1400\) and \(4\%\) sales tax will be added.
    1. How much money do you pay in sales tax?
      🔗
      🔗
    2. What is the total amount you pay, including sales tax?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    1. \(0.04 \times 1400\) dollars is paid in sales tax, which we can simplify to \(56\) dollars.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The total amount paid, including sales tax, is \(1400 + 0.04 \times 1400\) dollars, which we can also write as \((1 + 0.04)1400\) dollars, or \(1.04 \cdot 1400\) dollars, or \(1400 + 56\) dollars, or \(1456\) dollars.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Note 1.8.3.

    The initial expression for the final amount paid is \(1400 + 0.04 \cdot 1400\text{,}\) which mentions the original price \(1400\) twice. To touch on what Principle 1.8.1 is discussing, we are taking \(4\%\) of \(1400\) in this question.
    🔗
    🔗
    Before looking at another example, it is very important to see that the unsimplified and original expression for the total amount paid
    \begin{equation*} 1400 + 0.04 \cdot 1400 \end{equation*}
    mentions the original price of \(1400\) dollars twice. The amount of tax paid (in this case \(56\) dollars) is proportional to the subtotal of \(1400\) dollars. So, to come up with the number \(56\text{,}\) we had to compute \(0.04 \cdot 1400\text{.}\) Similarly, the total paid on an item that is originally priced at \(100\) dollars that has a \(4\%\) sales tax is \(100 + 0.04 \cdot 100\) dollars: in this case, the amount of tax paid is \(0.04 \cdot 100\) dollars, which is \(4\) dollars. (A less expensive item has lower tax, assuming that the tax rate of \(4\%\) remains the same.)
    🔗

    Example 1.8.4.

    An espresso machine has a base price of \(130\) dollars, but a sales tax of \(6\%\) must also be paid at checkout.
    • How much money is paid in sales tax?
      🔗
      🔗
    • What is the total amount paid, including sales tax?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    • The amount paid in sales tax is \(0.06 \cdot 130\) dollars, which we can simplify to \(7.80\) dollars.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The total amount paid, including sales tax, is \(130 + 0.06 \cdot 130\) dollars, which we can also write as \((1 + 0.06)130\) dollars, or \(1.06 \cdot 130\) dollars, or \(130 + 7.80\) dollars, or \(137.80\) dollars.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Note 1.8.5.

    It is extremely important to point out that the original expression in part 2 is \(130 + 0.06 \cdot 130\text{,}\) which is where all of the other answers (including \(137.80\)) come from. The specific thing to pay attention to is that the original price of \(130\) dollars appears twice in the first expression. This actually makes sense: we would have paid \(130\) dollars if there was no sales tax, but because of the sales tax, we are paying more (and the amount that we are paying more is proportional to the original price, and so it makes sense that the amount we are adding also involves the original price of \(130\) dollars).
    🔗
    As an example of Principle 1.8.1, we are taking \(6\%\) of \(130\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.8.6.

    A laptop has a price of \(L\) and \(4\%\) sales tax will be added.
    1. How much money do you pay in sales tax?
      🔗
      🔗
    2. What is the total amount you pay, including sales tax?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    1. You pay \(0.04 \times L\) dollars in sales tax.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The total amount paid, including sales tax, is \(L + 0.04 \times L\) dollars. There are, of course, additional ways to write this amount, but \(L + 0.04 \times L\) really captures what’s going on.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Note 1.8.7.

    The initial expression for the final amount paid is \(L + 0.04 \times L\text{,}\) which mentions the original price \(L\) twice.
    🔗
    As an example of Principle 1.8.1, we are taking \(4\%\) of an unknown quantity called \(L\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.8.8.

    A shirt has a price of \(\$21.50\) but a \(20\%\) discount is being offered.
    1. How much money do you save off the original price?
      🔗
      🔗
    2. What is the amount you actually pay for the shirt?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    1. The amount of discount is \(20\%\) of \(\$21.50\text{,}\) which is \(0.20 \times 21.50\) dollars, which we can simplify to \(4.30\) dollars.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The amount you actually pay is the original price minus the discount, which is \(21.50 - 0.20 \times 21.50\) dollars. We can also write this as \(21.50 - 4.30\) dollars, or going back to the original expression, we can factor out \(21.50\) and write as \((1 - 0.20)21.50\) dollars, or even \(0.80(21.50)\) dollars. Finally, we can take any of the above and fully simplify the final answer to \(17.20\) dollars.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Note 1.8.9.

    It is extremely important to point out that the original expression in part 2 is \(21.50 - 0.20 \times 21.50\text{,}\) which is where all of the other answers (including \(17.20\)) come from. The specific thing to pay attention to is that the original price of \(21.50\) dollars appears twice in the first expression. This actually makes sense: we would have paid \(21.50\) dollars if there was no discount, but because of the discount, we are paying less (and the amount that we are paying less is proportional to the original price, and so it makes sense that the amount we are subtracting also involves the original price of \(21.50\) dollars).
    🔗
    As an example of Principle 1.8.1, we are taking \(20\%\) percent of \(21.50\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.8.10.

    A toaster has a price of \(\$8.40\) but a \(8\%\) discount is being offered.
    1. How much money do you save off the original price?
      🔗
      🔗
    2. What is the amount you actually pay for the toaster?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    1. \(0.08 \times 8.40\) dollars, which we can simplify to \(0.672\) dollars.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(8.40 - 0.08 \times 8.40\) dollars, which we can also write as \((1 - 0.08)8.40\) dollars, or \(0.92(8.40)\) dollarsor \(8.40 - 0.672\) dollars or \(7.728\) dollars.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Note 1.8.11.

    The initial expression in part 2 is \(8.40 - 0.08 \times 8.40\text{,}\) which mentions \(8.40\) twice. It is tempting to skip to the other answers, but it is important to understand where they come from: \(8.40 - 0.08 \times 8.40\) is the original expression, and the original price \(8.40\) appears twice.
    🔗
    As an example of Principle 1.8.1, we are taking \(8\%\) of \(8.40\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.8.12.

    A hat has a price of \(h\) dollars but a \(6\%\) discount is being offered.
    1. How much money do you save off the original price?
      🔗
      🔗
    2. What is the amount you actually pay for the hat?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    1. You save \(0.06 \times h\) dollars. This cannot be simplified any further because we do not know the value of \(h\text{.}\) (Note that in Part 1 of Example 1.8.10, we multiplied the decimal version of the percentage \(0.08\) by the original amount of \(8.40\) to get \(0.08 \cdot 8.40\text{.}\) In this problem, we also multiply the decimal version of the percentage \(0.06\) by the original amount of \(h\) to get \(0.06 \cdot h\text{.}\) Conceptually, we are doing the same thing, but the difference is that in this problem, the original amount is represented by a variable \(h\) rather than a constant like \(8.40\text{.}\))
      🔗
      🔗
    2. You pay \(h - 0.06 \times h\) dollars, which we can also write as \((1 - 0.06)h\) dollars, or \(0.94h\) dollars. (Note that in Part 2 of Example 1.8.10, we subtracted the amount saved from the original price to get the final price. In this problem, we are also subtracting the amount saved from the original amount of \(h\) to get the final price.)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Note 1.8.13.

    Just like in Note 1.8.9 and Note 1.8.11, the initial expression in part 2 mentions the original price twice. In this case, the initial expression in part 2 is \(h - 0.06 \times h\text{,}\) which mentions \(h\) twice. Especially in the case of an unknown original price, the original price (represented by a variable, like \(h\) in the previous example) will appear twice in the initial expression.
    🔗
    As an example of Principle 1.8.1, we are taking \(6\) percent of \(h\text{.}\)

    Example 1.8.16.

    You will transfer \(M\) miles for a flight. Your credit card usually transfers miles at a \(1:1\) ratio, but is currently offering a \(10\%\) bonus on all transfers.
    1. How many bonus miles do you receive?
      🔗
      🔗
    2. What is the total number of miles you will have after the transfer and bonus?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    1. You receive \(0.10 \times M\) bonus miles.
      🔗

    Note 1.8.17.

    Examine how Part 1 of this example and Part 1 of the previous example are similar. Likewise, notice how Part 2 of this example is a parallel to Part 2 of the previous example.
    🔗
    🔗
    Let’s examine a series of examples which combine taxes and tips, which is extremely common in restaurant settings.
    🔗

    Example 1.8.18.

    A restaurant meal has a price of \(\$14.80\text{.}\) A \(5\%\) tax must be paid, and \(15\%\) tip will be included on the post-tax amount.
    1. How much money is paid in tax?
      🔗
      🔗
    2. How much total money is paid for the meal and tax?
      🔗
      🔗
    3. How much total money is paid in tip?
      🔗
      🔗
    4. How much total money is paid in meal, tax, and tip?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    1. \(0.05 \cdot 14.80\) dollars is paid in tax.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(14.80 + 0.05 \cdot 14.80\) dollars represents the amount paid for the meal and the tax. (This is the post-tax amount, which will be needed for the next part.)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The post-tax amount is \(14.80 + 0.05 \cdot 14.80\) dollars. To compute the amount of tip to pay, we need to take \(15\%\) of this post-tax amount. In other words, the amount paid in tip is \(0.15 \cdot (14.80 + 0.05 \cdot 14.80)\) dollars.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. We have to pay for all three parts. To name the pieces (and how much each piece is), we pay for the meal (which is \(14.80\) dollars), and we pay for the tax (which is \(0.05 \cdot 14.80\) dollars), and we pay for the tip (which is \(0.15 \cdot (14.80 + 0.05 \cdot 14.80)\) dollars). Since we have to pay for all three parts, we add these together to get the total amount paid: \(14.80 + 0.05 \cdot 14.80 + 0.15 \cdot (14.80 + 0.05 \cdot 14.80)\) dollars.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Questions that involve two percentages can be confusing. Let’s think of \(14.80\) as the “normal price”. Instead of paying the normal price, we first see the need to pay a tax of \(0.05 \cdot 14.80\text{,}\) but we can’t just pay the tax: we have to pay for the meal and the tax. To speak informally, let’s call \(14.80 + 0.05 \cdot 14.80\) the “affected price”. The important point to make here is that we need to then take \(15\%\) of the affected price instead of \(15\%\) of the “normal price”.
    🔗

    Example 1.8.19.

    A restaurant meal has a price of \(M\text{.}\) A \(5\%\) tax must be paid, and \(15\%\) tip will be included on the post-tax amount.
    1. How much money is paid in tax?
      🔗
      🔗
    2. How much total money is paid for the meal and tax?
      🔗
      🔗
    3. How much total money is paid in tip?
      🔗
      🔗
    4. How much total money is paid in meal, tax, and tip?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    1. The tax to pay is \(0.05 \cdot M\) dollars.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The post-tax amount is \(M + 0.05 \cdot M\) dollars, which is the sum of the meal and the tax.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The tip is \(15\%\) of the post-tax amount from the previous part. So, the tip is \(0.15 \cdot (M + 0.05 \cdot M)\) dollars.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. The total paid is \(M + 0.05 \cdot M + 0.15 \cdot (M + 0.05 \cdot M)\) dollars.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Note 1.8.20.

    It is important to really pay close attention to how similar the answers to the four parts in this example are in the same format of the answers to the four parts in the previous example. Each time the previous question had the number \(14.80\text{,}\) this question has the variable \(M\) instead.
    🔗
    🔗
    This question is more challenging than the other questions we’ve seen. We end up needing to take \(15\%\) of the post-tax amount, which is typically how tips are calculated in restaurants. Principle 1.8.1 reminds us that we should always ask what we’re taking \(15\%\) of. In this example, we need \(15\%\) of the post-tax amount, and the post-tax amount was \(M + 0.05 \cdot M\text{,}\) which is why we wrote \(0.15 \cdot (M + 0.05 \cdot M)\) in the expression for the amount of tip paid.
    🔗

    Example 1.8.21.

    A game store has a board game available for \(40\) dollars. The store is offering \(5\%\) off, but based on the discount price, there is a \(5\%\) tax that needs to be paid. What is the final amount paid?
    🔗
    Answer.
    The store is taking off \(5\%\) of \(40\) dollars. So, if there was no tax, the amount to pay is \(40 - 0.05 \cdot 40\) dollars. However, based on this amount of \(40 - 0.05 \cdot 40\text{,}\) we have to pay \(5\%\) of \(40 - 0.05 \cdot 40\) as a tax. That is, the amount of tax we need to pay is \(0.05(40 - 0.05 \cdot 40)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Thus, the total to pay is \(40 - 0.05 \cdot 40 + 0.05(40 - 0.05 \cdot 40)\text{.}\) Using a calculator, this is equal to \(39.90\) dollars.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Equations involving percentages

    We now build on the previous examples that just involved expressions. The expressions that we built will end up being one side of equations that we build and then solve. One of the most powerful aspects of algebra is that we can represent an unknown quantity with a variable, and that variable can appear more than once in an equation. It is tempting to try to ignoe the algebra processes and try to build our own recipes for doing arithmetic only, but there is danger in doing this. Writing an equation with a variable is meant to naturally write about the situations that we encounter.
    🔗

    Principle 1.8.22. Variables.

    A variable can be created to represent an unknown quantity in a problem. It is important to always keep track of what each created variable represents, and apply that meaning consistently throughout our process.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.8.23.

    You have allocated \(1,600\) dollars to buy a laptop, but have to also pay a \(4\%\) sales tax. What is the pre-tax price of a laptop that you can afford?
    🔗
    Answer.
    Let’s have \(L\) represent the pre-tax price (in dollars) of the laptop. In Example 1.8.6, we wrote \(L + 0.04 \cdot L\) to represent the total dollar amount paid for the laptop, including sales tax. Information that wasn’t in the earlier question (since it wasn’t needed then) but is included here is that \(1,600\) dollars is the total amount spent on the laptop. Both \(L + 0.04 \cdot L\) and \(1,600\) represent the same concept: namely, the total amount spent on the laptop. In the question here, we have the additional information that the total amount spent on the laptop is \(1,600\) dollars. Therefore, we can build an equation by setting these two expressions equal to each other:
    \begin{equation*} L + 0.04 \cdot L = 1,600. \end{equation*}
    Since \(L\) represents the pre-tax price, and this is what the question asked about, we will solve for \(L\text{.}\) There are various things that someone may wish to emphasize in solving this equation, so we’ll present several options:
    • From \(L + 0.04 \cdot L = 1600\) we can factor out \(L\) on the left side to get
      \begin{equation*} L(1 + 0.04) = 1600. \end{equation*}
      Dividing both sides by \(1 + 0.04\text{,}\) we get
      \begin{equation*} L = \frac{1600}{1 + 0.04}. \end{equation*}
      If there was no requirement to simplify, we can say that the pre-tax price of the laptop is \(\frac{1600}{1 + 0.04}\) dollars. If we need to simplify, typing 1600 / (1 + 0.04) into a calculator gives approximately \(1538.46\) dollars.
      🔗
      🔗
    • From \(L + 0.04 \cdot L = 1600\) we can collect like terms on the left side to get
      \begin{equation*} 1.04 L = 1600. \end{equation*}
      Dividing both sides by \(1.04\text{,}\) we get
      \begin{equation*} L = \frac{1600}{1.04}. \end{equation*}
      If there was no requirement to simplify, we can say that the pre-tax price of the laptop is \(\frac{1600}{1.04}\) dollars. If we need to simplify, typing 1600 / 1.04 into a calculator gives approximately \(1538.46\) dollars.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Note 1.8.24.

    Though it was said implicitly earlier, let’s just make clear that in the equtation
    \begin{equation*} L + 0.04L = 1600 \end{equation*}
    exactly what’s happening. The left side has two terms. The first term \(L\) represents the dollar amount of the pre-tax price of the laptop. The second term represents the dollar amount of taxes paid. Since both of these things need to get paid, we add these together, to represent the dollar amount of the total paid. The right side, \(1600\text{,}\) represents the total dollar amount that can be spent on the laptop.
    🔗
    The temptation is to try to examine only a more simplied version of this equation such as
    \begin{equation*} 1.04L = 1600 \end{equation*}
    and then try to say something like “when we see \(4\) percent, then the number \(1.04\) must appear” but this is not not always true, and it’s hiding what’s really happening.

    Warning 1.8.25.

    There is a temptation to state that the final answer should be \(1600 - 0.04 \cdot 1600\) dollars. There are several things we should say about this as an answer. As usual, we don’t just jump to quick words to say “oh, don’t do this”. It’s good to first focus on analyzing the answer given here without first bringing up a different answer.
    • Let’s articulate what \(1600 - 0.04 \cdot 1600\) represents. We are starting with \(1600\text{,}\) then subtracting off something. The amount that is being subtracted off is \(4\%\) of \(1600\text{.}\) So, this expression represents what would be paid for a product that originally has a price of \(1600\) but then a \(4\%\) discount applied.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The key thing to note in \(1600 - 0.04 \cdot 1600\) is to see what we’re taking \(4\%\) of, a concept we introduced in Principle 1.8.1. Writing \(1600 - 0.04 \cdot 1600\) means that we’re taking \(4\%\) of \(1600\text{,}\) but \(1600\) represents the total (final) amount paid, but we instead need to compute \(4\%\) of the pre-tax price.
      🔗
      🔗
    • It is true that we need \(4\%\) of the pre-tax price, and at the beginning of the qustion, we don’t know what the pre-tax price is. The pre-tax price was represented by the variable \(L\text{.}\) So, to express \(4\%\) of the pre-tax price, we write \(0.04 \cdot L\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Finally, we should note that \(\frac{1600}{1 + 0.04}\) and \(1600 - 0.04 \cdot 1600\) are different in a calculator. That is to say: it’s not the case that arrving at \(1600 - 0.04 \cdot 1600\) provides an alternative approach to thinking abou the question. That process doesn’t work out. Instead, we really do need to think about Principle 1.8.1. So, if we need to take \(4\%\) of an unknown amount, then we end up writing \(0.04\) times a variable.

    Subsection Systems of equations

    We will now look at two techniques for solving sysstems of equations. By systems of equations, we mean that there will be more than one equation, and typically, each equation will have several variables. This is what happens in general, but we will stick to two equations which each have the variables \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 1.8.30.

    Solve the system of equations \(2x+y=1\) and \(3x+4y=14\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    We can solve for any of the two variables in either equation. For example, we can solve the first equation for \(y\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} y = 1 - 2x \end{equation*}
    Now, we will write the equation \(3x+4y=14\) with the one exception that \(y\) is replaced with \(1 - 2x\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} 3x + 4(1 - 2x) = 14 \end{equation*}
    What we just did was replace \(y\) with something that is equal to \(y\) that we discovered earlier. The new equation that we just got no longer has a mix of \(x\) and \(y\text{;}\) it only has \(x\text{.}\) Now we take that equation and solve for \(x\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} 3x + 4 - 8x = 14 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} -5x + 4 = 14 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} -5x = 10 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = -2 \end{equation*}
    Now we can substitute this value of \(x\) in \(y = 1 - 2x\) to get:
    \begin{equation*} y = 1 - 2(-2) = 5 \end{equation*}
    So the solution to the system of equations is \((x,y)=(-2, 5)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Note 1.8.31.

    The phrasing of the question was “Solve the system of equations \(2x+y=1\) and \(3x+4y=14\)” but we could have also said “Find values for \(x\) and \(y\) that satisfy both \(2x+y=1\) and \(3x+4y=14\)”. Both phrasings are equivalent. The point is that \((x,y)=(-3,7)\) satisfies the first equation \(2x+y=1\text{,}\) but not the second equation \(3x+4y=14\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The solution \((x,y)=(-2,5)\) satisfies both equations simultaneously: that is, if we substitute \(x=-2\) and \(y=5\) into each both equations, the left side equals the right side.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.8.32.

    Solve the system of equations: \(2x-y=5\) and \(x+4y=7\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer 1.
    We can solve for any of the two variables in either equation. For example, we can solve the first equation for \(y\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} 2x-5 = y \end{equation*}
    Now, we will write the equation \(x+4y=7\) with the one exception that \(y\) is replaced with \(2x - 5\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} x + 4(2x - 5) = 7 \end{equation*}
    The new equation that we just got no longer has a mix of \(x\) and \(y\text{;}\) it only has \(x\text{.}\) Now we take that equation and solve for \(x\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} x + 8x - 20 = 7 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 9x - 20 = 7 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 9x = 27 \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation*} y = 2(3) - 5 = 1 \end{equation*}
    So the solution to the system of equations is \((x,y)=(3, 1)\text{.}\)

    Answer 2.
    We can start by solving for \(x\) in the second equation instead:
    \begin{equation*} x = 7 - 4y \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation*} 2(7 - 4y) - y = 5 \end{equation*}

    Subsection Distance, rate, and time

    The following formula is applicable in questions about distance, rate, and time:
    🔗

    Principle 1.8.34. Distance is the product of rate and time.

    \((\text{distance}) = (\text{rate}) \times (\text{time})\)
    🔗
    🔗
    In the formula above, the word rate is often used, but the word could have been speed or velocity instead. The units for each of the three parts need to be compatible with each other. For example, if the distance is in miles and the time is in hours, then the rate must be in miles per hour. Similarly, if the distance is in meters and the time is in seconds, then the rate is in meters per second.
    🔗

    Note 1.8.35. Clarification.

    In questions that about distance, rate, and time, there are often two trips. The two trips might be two different vehicles going towards each other, or two vehicles traveling away from each other, or even two vehicles traveling in the same direction. In addition, the two trips might just all be two legs of the same overall journey: for example, someone might drive part of the way at one speed, and then take a bus from a parking lot to their final destination at a different speed. It is helpful to identify the two trips. I encourage you to articulate each trip using more than a single word, especially if it’s one person involved in both trips.
    🔗
    Each trip has a distance, earch trip has a rate, and each trip has a time. When we have question involving two trips, here are some guidelines about distance, rate, and time. The following lists don’t give the only possibilities, but these are the most frequent situations:
    • Distance
      • The two trips might have the same distance.
        🔗
        🔗
      • The distances from the two trips might add up to a total known distance.
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    • Time
      • The two trips might have the same timee.
        🔗
        🔗
      • The times from the two trips might add up to a total known time.
        🔗
        🔗
      • The time for one trip might be obtained from the time for the other trip by adding or subtracting a known amount of time.
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    • Rate
      • The two trips might have the same rate.
        🔗
        🔗
      • The rate for one trip might be obtained from the rate for the other trip by multiplying or dividing by a known factor.
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.8.36.

    Two cyclists, \(63\) miles apart, start riding toward each other at the same time. One cycles twice as fast as the other. If they meet \(2\) hours later, at what average speed is each cyclist traveling?
    🔗
    Answer.
    First, let’s carefully identify the two trips. One trip is the journey made by the first cyclist. The second trip is the journey made by the second cyclist. We don’t have much that distinguishes the two cyclists from each other, except that one is traveling faster than the other. Let’s say that the first trip is the trip made by the slower cyclist, and the second trip is the trip made by the faster cyclist. Both trips have the same time, which is \(2\) hours.
    🔗
    Let’s use \(r\) to represent the rate (in miles per hour) of the slower cyclist. Then, the rate of the faster cyclist is \(2r\) miles per hour. (This is an example of when the rate for one trip is obtained from the rate for the other trip by multiplying by a known factor.)
    🔗
    We have \(r\) for the rate of the first trip and \(2\) for the time of the first trip, so we can mulitply these together to get \(2r\) for the distance of the first trip. Similarly, we have \(2r\) for the rate of the second trip and \(2\) for the time of the second trip, so we can mulitply these together to get \(2 \cdot 2r\) which is \(4r\) for the distance of the second trip.
    🔗
    In this question, the two cyclist ride towards each other starting \(63\) miles apart, and this experiment ends when they meet each other. Thus, this is an example of a situation where the two distances from the two trips add up to a total known distance. Therefore, we can write the equation:
    \begin{equation*} 2r + 4r = 63. \end{equation*}
    Combining like terms on the left side gives:
    \begin{equation*} 6r = 63. \end{equation*}
    Dividing both sides by \(6\text{,}\) we get:
    \begin{equation*} r = \frac{63}{6}. \end{equation*}
    The rate of the slower cyclist is \(\frac{63}{6}\) miles per hour, which simplifies to \(10.5\) miles per hour. The rate of the faster cyclist is \(2r = 2 \cdot \frac{63}{6} = \frac{126}{6}\) miles per hour, which simplifies to \(21\) miles per hour.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    We had made the choice to write \(r\) for the rate of the slower cyclist and \(2r\) for the rate of the faster cyclist. We could have made the opposite choice, writing \(r\) for the rate of the faster cyclist and \(\frac{r}{2}\) for the rate of the slower cyclist. This would have led to a different equation, but solving that equation would have led to the same final answers for the rates of the two cyclists. We made the choice to have \(r\) represent the rate of the slower cyclist because it made the equation a little simpler by not introducing fractions. The key thing was to be clear: we shouldn’t just say “\(r\) is the rate.” Instead, said “\(r\) is the rate of the slower cyclist.”
    🔗

    Example 1.8.37.

    Wendy took a trip whose total distance was \(135\) miles. She traveled part of the way by bus, which arrived at the train station just in time for Wendy to complete her journey by train. The bus averaged \(25\) miles per hour, and the train averaged \(60\) miles per hour. The entire trip took \(4\) hours. How long did Wendy spend on the train?
    🔗

    Example 1.8.38.

    One car leaves from Town A to Town B at a speed of \(50\) miles per hour. Another car leaves from Town A one hour later at a speed of \(70\) miles per hour. If the two cars arrive at Town B at the same time, what is the distance between the two towns?
    🔗
    Answer 1.
    Let’s identify the two trips. The first trip is the journey made by the first car. The second trip is the journey made by the second car. The first trip has rate \(50\) miles per hour, and the second trip has rate \(70\) miles per hour. We don’t know the time of either trip, so let’s create variables: \(x\) will represent the time (in hours) spent on the first trip and \(y\) will represent the time (in hours) spent on the second trip. Because the second car leaves one hour later but both cars arrive at Town B at the same time, this is an example where the time for one trip is obtained from the time for the other trip by adding a known amount of time. Thus, we can write the equation:
    \begin{equation*} x = y + 1. \end{equation*}
    Before going any further, let’s make clear why we have \(x = y + 1\) instead of \(y = x + 1\text{.}\) The thing is, the first car gets to travel an extra hour more than the second car, so this is why the value of \(x\) has to be larger than the value of \(y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Since the first trip has a rate of \(50\) and a time of \(x\text{,}\) we can multiply these together to get \(50x\) for the distance of the first trip. Since the second trip has a rate of \(70\) and a time of \(y\text{,}\) we can multiply these together to get \(70y\) for the distance of the second trip. Because both cars arrive at Town B, the distances for the two trips are the same, so we can write the equation:
    \begin{equation*} 50x = 70y. \end{equation*}
    Since we earlier had \(x = y + 1\text{,}\) we can replace every \(x\) with \(y+1\) in the equation \(50x = 70y\) to get:
    \begin{equation*} 50(y + 1) = 70y. \end{equation*}
    (Please note that when we replaced \(x\) with \(y+1\text{,}\) we wrote the left side to be \(50(y+1)\) with emphasis on the parentheses because we really need the rate \(50\) to be multiplied by the time \(y+1\text{.}\) If we wrote \(50 \cdot y + 1\) without parentheses instead, what is written is not a product accourding to the Order of Operations.) Now we take that equation and solve for \(y\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} 50y + 50 = 70y \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 50 = 20y \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \frac52 = y \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We have the value of \(y\) which represents the time in hours for the second trip. However, the question asked about the distance between the two towns. We have both \(50x\) and \(70y\) as representations for the distance, but since we have the value of \(y\text{,}\) it will be more convenient for us to think of the distance as \(70y\text{.}\) So, the distance between the two towns is \(70y = 70 \cdot \frac52 = 175\) miles.

    Example 1.8.39.

    One car leaves from Town A to Town B at a speed of \(60\) miles per hour. The return ride from Town B to Town A is made at a speed of \(40\) miles per hour due to heavy traffic, and took \(2\) hours longer than the trip from Town A to Town B. What was the total time spent driving?
    🔗
    Answer.
    The first trip is the ride from Town A to Town B, and the second trip is the ride back. The first trip has rate \(60\) miles per hour, and the second trip has rate \(40\) miles per hour. We don’t know the amount of time for either of the trips. We cannot use \(x\) to represent the time of both trips. Here are some options:
    • We could use \(x\) to represent the time for the first trip, and then the time for the second trip would be \(x+2\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • We could use \(x\) to represent the time for the first trip, and another variable \(y\) to represent the time for the second trip, and then we would have the equation \(y = x + 2\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • We could introduce a variable \(c\) to represent the time for the second trip, and then the time for the first trip would be \(c-2\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • We could use \(c\) to represent the time for the second trip, and another variable \(d\) to represent the time for the first trip, and then we would have the equation \(d = c - 2\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Any one of these four options would work, as long as we stuck kept track of what each and every variable means. Let’s go ahead with the first option. Then, the distance for the first trip is \(60x\) and the distance for the second trip is \(40(x+2)\text{.}\) Both trips must cover the same distance (because going from B to A should cover the same distance as going from A to B). So, we can set these two distances equal to create our equation:
    \begin{equation*} 60x = 40(x+2) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 60x = 40x + 80 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 20x = 80 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = 4 \end{equation*}

    Subsection Mixture

    We will examine questions where one or more containers contain liquid (which can also be referred to as fluid or solution). In each container, we will typically have the following situation: a container will be filled with a certain amount of liquid (which we call the amount of solution) but some fraction of that liquid (which we call the concentration) will be a specially-identified liquid, which will often be acid. To be clear, we mean to say that a container will have some liquid that is acid as well as some liquid that is not acid.
    🔗

    Example 1.8.40.

    I have a beaker (Beaker 1) with \(8\) liters of fluid. If \(50\%\) of the fluid in the beaker is acid, how many liters of acid are in the beaker?
    🔗
    Answer.
    Because the concentration is \(50\%\text{,}\) this means that have of the liquid in the container is acid. Since the container has \(8\) total liters of liquid, we conclude that there are \(4\) liters of acid in Beaker 1.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Principle 1.8.41. Concentration equation.

    \((\text{amount solution})(\text{concentration}) = (\text{amount acid})\)
    🔗
    When applying this formula, the concentration should be a fraction/decimal between \(0\) and \(1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The exercise we already worked through is actually an example of this concentration equation: we had \(8\) total liters of solution, and the concentration turned into a decimal is \(0.50\text{.}\) And following the concentration equation,
    \begin{equation*} (\text{amount solution})(\text{concentration}) = (\text{amount acid}) \end{equation*}
    turns into
    \begin{equation*} 8 \cdot 0.50 = 4 \end{equation*}
    This concentration equation can be used on every container. In fact, this concentration equation should be used on each container in a mixture problem. Before we dig any further, let’s lead up to ideas with a couple more questions as warm ups.
    🔗

    Example 1.8.42.

    Beaker 2 contains \(12\) liters of liquid. If \(75\%\) of the liquid is acid, how many liters of acid are in Beaker 2?
    🔗
    Answer.
    We apply the concentration equation with the amount of solution \(12\) and the concentration \(0.75\text{.}\) So
    \begin{equation*} (\text{amount solution})(\text{concentration}) = (\text{amount acid}) \end{equation*}
    turns into
    \begin{equation*} 12 \cdot 0.75 = (\text{amount acid}) \end{equation*}
    and using a calculator, we see that there will be \(9\) liters of acid.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Though we are completely done with this question, let’s just point out that in this container that had \(12\) liters of total liquid, \(9\) of the liters are acid, meaning that the remaining \(3\) liters is non-acid liquid.

Then, what would be the concentration for the bucket? What would we get if we added the two concertations together? Well, \(50\%\) plus \(75\%\) would be \(125\%\text{,}\) so something seems incorrect about this awfully large percent.

Beaker 1 had \(8\) liters of liquid, of which \(4\) liters were acid (meaning the remaining \(4\) liters are non-acid).     Example 1.8.43.

    Beaker 1 contains \(8\) liters of liquid, and \(50\%\) of the liquid in the beaker is acid. Beaker 2 contains \(12\) liters of liquid, and \(75\%\) of the liquid is acid. The contents of Beaker 1 and Beaker 2 are dumped into an empty bucket. What percentage of the bucket is acid? (That is, what is the concentration of the bucket?)
    🔗
    Answer.
    The bucket will have \(20\) total liters of liquid, coming from \(8 + 12\text{.}\) The bucket will have \(13\) total liters of acid, coming from \(4 + 9\text{,}\) since we recall from earlier that Beaker 1 had \(4\) liters of acid, and Beaker 2 had \(9\) liters of acid. So, for the bucket,
    \begin{equation*} (\text{amount solution})(\text{concentration}) = (\text{amount acid}) \end{equation*}
    turns into
    \begin{equation*} 20 \cdot (\text{concentration}) = 13 \end{equation*}
    The question asked about the concentration, so we divide both sides by \(20\) to get
    \begin{equation*} \text{concentration} = \frac{13}{20} \end{equation*}
    While we can leave our answer like this, a calculator tells us the concentration is \(0.65\text{,}\) and we can restate the concentration of the bucket to be \(65\%\text{.}\)

    Example 1.8.48.

    How many liters of a \(20\%\) acid solution should be mixed with \(80\) liters of a \(61\%\) acid solution to make a \(45\%\) acid solution?
    🔗
    Answer.
    Let’s say that Beaker 1 is the container with the \(20\%\) acid solution, and Beaker 2 is the container with the \(61\%\) acid solution. We don’t know how many liters are in Beaker 1, so let’s introduce a variable \(x\) to represent the amount of solution (in liters) in Beaker 1. The amount of solution in Beaker 2 is \(80\) liters. The concentration in Beaker 1 is \(0.20\text{,}\) and the concentration in Beaker 2 is \(0.61\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We need to apply the concentration equation to Beaker 1, and this tells us the amount of acid in Beaker 1 is \(0.20x\text{.}\) We need to apply the concentration equation to Beaker 2, and this tells us the amount of acid in Beaker 2 is \(0.61(80)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The amount of solution in the bucket is the sum of the amounts of soultion in Beaker 1 and Beaker 2, so the amount of solution in the bucket is \(x + 80\text{.}\) The concentration in the bucket is \(0.45\text{.}\) Applying the concentration equation to the bucket tells us that the amount of acid in the bucket is \(0.45(x + 80)\text{.}\) The amount of acid in the bucket is the sum of the amounts of acid in Beaker 1 and Beaker 2, so the amount of acid in the bucket is \(0.20x + 0.61(80)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We now have two expressions for the amount of acid in the bucket, so we can set these equal to each other:
    \begin{equation*} 0.45(x + 80) = 0.20x + 0.61(80) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 0.45x + 36 = 0.20x + 48.8 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 0.25x = 12.8 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = 51.2 \end{equation*}
    So, we need to mix \(51.2\) liters of the \(20\%\) acid solution with \(80\) liters of the \(61\%\) acid solution to make a \(45\%\) acid solution.

    🔗

    \begin{equation*} 0.45(x + 80) = 0.20x + 0.61(80) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 0.45x + 0.45(80) = 0.20x + 0.61(80) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 0.45x - 0.20x = 0.61(80) - 0.45(80) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (0.45-0.20)x = 0.61(80) - 0.45(80) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = \frac{0.61(80) - 0.45(80)}{0.45-0.20} \end{equation*}
    So we need to have \(\frac{0.61(80) - 0.45(80)}{0.45-0.20}\) liters of the \(20\%\) acid solution.
    🔗

    Strategy 1.8.50.

    In these mixture problems, there will be two different ways to obtain the amount of acid in the bucket:
    • Multiply the amount of solution in the bucket by the concentration of the bucket.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Add the amount of acid in Beaker 1 to the amount of acid in Beaker 2.
      🔗
      🔗
    There will always be these two different ways to obtain the amount of acid in the bucket. These will always be these two different-looking expressions for the amount of acid in the bucket. These will look different because one is a product while the other is a sum. Though these look different, both represent the amount of acid in the bucket, so we will always set these two expressions equal to each other to create the relevant equation. For every problem where the contents of two beakers is dumped into an empty bucket, this will always end up being how we create the relevant equation.
    🔗
    🔗
    The methodology we described will always apply, but in the details, things maay look a little different from one problem to the next. So, let’s look for which patterns are always present, and avoid discovering patterns which only work sometimes.
    🔗

    Example 1.8.51.

    How many liters of a \(20\%\) acid solution and how many liters of a \(61\%\) acid solution should be mixed together to make \(80\) liters of a \(45\%\) acid solution?
    🔗
    Answer 1.
    This problem has all the same numbers as in the previous problem, but some of the numbers appear in different places. Beaker 1 has a concentration of \(0.20\) and Beaker 2 has a concentration of \(0.61\text{.}\) We don’t have the total amount of solution for either beaker, but the beakers combined have \(80\) liters of solution. Let’s write \(x\) for the amount of solution in Beaker 1, which means that \(80-x\) liters of solution are in Beaker 2.
    🔗
    The amount of acid in Beaker 1 is \(0.20x\) and the amount of acid in Beaker 2 is \(0.61(80-x)\text{.}\) We can get the amount of acid in the bucket two ways: one is by adding the amounts of acid from the beakers, so we have \(0.20x + 0.61(80-x)\text{,}\) and the other way is by multiplying the desired concentration \(0.45\) by the amount of fluid \(80\) to get \(0.45(80)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We set equal these two ways of writing the amount of acid in the bucket:
    \begin{equation*} 0.20x + 0.61(80-x) = 0.45(80) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 0.20x + 0.61(80) - 0.61x = 0.45(80) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 0.20x - 0.61x = 0.45(80) - 0.61(80) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (0.20 - 0.61)x = 0.45(80) - 0.61(80) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = \frac{0.45(80) - 0.61(80)}{0.20 - 0.61} \end{equation*}
    We need \(\frac{0.45(80) - 0.61(80)}{0.20 - 0.61}\) liters of the first solution. Since \(80-x\) represents the number of liters of the second solution, we need \(80 - \frac{0.45(80) - 0.61(80)}{0.20 - 0.61}\) liters of the second solution.
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer 2.
    This problem has all the same numbers as in the previous problem, but some of the numbers appear in different places. Beaker 1 has a concentration of \(0.20\) and Beaker 2 has a concentration of \(0.61\text{.}\) We don’t have the total amount of solution for either beaker, but the beakers combined have \(80\) liters of solution. Let’s write \(x\) for the amount of solution in Beaker 1 and \(y\) for the amount of solution in Beaker 2. The previous answer had implemented that the sum should be \(80\) by writing \(80-x\text{.}\) I Instead, we implement this information by writing \(x+y=80\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The amount of acid in Beaker 1 is \(0.20x\) and the amount of acid in Beaker 2 is \(0.61y\text{.}\) We can get the amount of acid in the bucket two ways: one is by adding the amounts of acid from the beakers, so we have \(0.20x + 0.61y\text{,}\) and the other way is by multiplying the desired concentration \(0.45\) by the amount of fluid \(80\) to get \(0.45(80)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We set equal these two ways of writing the amount of acid in the bucket:
    \begin{equation*} 0.20x + 0.61y = 0.45(80) \end{equation*}
    From \(x+y=80\text{,}\) we get \(y=80-x\)
    \begin{equation*} 0.20x + 0.61(80-x) = 0.45(80) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 0.20x + 0.61(80) - 0.61x = 0.45(80) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 0.20x - 0.61x = 0.45(80) - 0.61(80) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (0.20 - 0.61)x = 0.45(80) - 0.61(80) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = \frac{0.45(80) - 0.61(80)}{0.20 - 0.61} \end{equation*}
    We need \(\frac{0.45(80) - 0.61(80)}{0.20 - 0.61}\) liters of the first solution. Since \(y\) represents the number of liters of the second solution, and \(y=80-x\text{,}\) we need \(80 - \frac{0.45(80) - 0.61(80)}{0.20 - 0.61}\) liters of the second solution.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Note 1.8.52.

    Both answers validly implement the information that the sum of the amounts of solutions needs to be \(80\text{.}\)
    • The first answer had \(x\) and \(80-x\) as the amounts of solution, since these two quantities add to \(80\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The second answer had \(x\) and \(y\) as the amounts of solution, and includes the equaiton \(x+y=80\) to create a system of equations.
      🔗
      🔗
    However, we should be careful to not do the following:
    • It is incorrect to say that both beakers have \(40\) as the amount of solution. While it is true that \(40+40=80\text{,}\) we cannot assume that both containers have the same amount of solution.
      🔗
      🔗
    • It is incorrect to say that both beakers have \(x\) as the amount of solution. Doing this assumes that both containers have the same amount of solution, because \(x\) was used to label the amount of solution for both containers.
      🔗
      🔗
    • It is incorrect to say that both beakers have \(80-x\) as the amount of solution. This, implicitly, is another way that the same value (namely \(80-x\)) appears as the amount of solution for both of the beakers. This is often the result of reading too much into the pattern: in our second answer to the previous question, one container had \(x\) for the amount of solution, while the other container had \(80-x\) as the amount of solution. The two expressions \(x\) and \(80-x\) add up to \(80\text{,}\) which you can verify experimentally by picking any number you want for \(x\text{,}\) then seeing what happens when you add \(x\) and \(80-x\) together, or we can provide the following argument where we don’t plug anything in for \(x\) and just simplify the sum of \(x\) and \((80-x)\text{,}\) obtaining:
      \begin{equation*} (x) + (80-x) = x + 80 - x = 80 \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    Note that in both answers, the variable \(x\) itself is meant to represent something, and in this case, \(x\) represents the amount of liquid in Beaker 1.

    Subsection Literal Equations

    Due to applications in science, it is very typical to need to take an equation that has multiple variables (often more than two) and solve for one of the variables in terms of the others. These are called literal equations, since the variables are often thought of as "letters" (or "literals") rather than specific unknown numbers. Before looking at examples, let us look at an equation that only has one variable, and provided two different-looking answers:
    🔗

    Example 1.8.53.

    Solve \(3x + 5 = 11\) for \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer 1.
    One way to solve for \(x\) is as follows:
    \begin{equation*} 3x + 5 = 11 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 3x = 6 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = 2 \end{equation*}
    After performing an action on both sides, we simplified where possible.
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer 2.
    Another way to solve for \(x\) is as follows:
    \begin{equation*} 3x + 5 = 11 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 3x = 11 - 5 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = \frac{11 - 5}{3} \end{equation*}
    After performing an action on both sides, we did not simplify.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Both answers are correct. The first answer simplified where possible, while the second answer did not simplify. Both answers correctly solved for \(x\text{.}\) It would be very natural to ask why we even provide the second answer. This is because literal equations often put us in situations where we cannot simplify.
    🔗

    Example 1.8.54.

    Solve \(ax + b = c\) for \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} ax + b = c \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} ax = c - b \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = \frac{c - b}{a} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Examine how similar the steps to solving this equation are like to the steps in the second answer of the previous example. While this process may feel strange at first, please note that the key steps are the same as solving equations with one variable: apply the same action to both sides. The thing that is different is that we are often unable to perform any simplification work after performing the action on both sides. (This is because the variables represent unknown numbers, and we cannot simplify without knowing what those numbers are.)
    🔗
    Earlier in this section we examined solving systems of equations. We in fact already practiced solving literal equations when we solved those systems of equations. For example, if we were asked to solve \(3x + y = 17\) and \(2x - 9y = 40\text{,}\) our first step might take the first equation and subtract \(3x\) from both sides to get \(y = 17 - 3x\text{,}\) noting that no simplification occured. We would then substitute this expression for \(y\) into the second equation to get \(2x - 9(17 - 3x) = 40\text{,}\) and then proceed to solve for \(x\text{.}\) We won’t continue on this example any further: this was just to illustrate that we have already dealt with multiple variables in an equation, and the specific situation of being unable to simplify further after performing an action on both sides.
    🔗

    Example 1.8.55.

    Solve \(w\) in the perimeter equation for a rectangle: \(P = 2\ell + 2w\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} P = 2\ell + 2w \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} P - 2\ell = 2w \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} w = \frac{P - 2\ell}{2} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.8.56.

    Solve for \(h\) in the surface area equation for a rectangular prism: \(A = 2\ell w + 2 \ell h + 2wh\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer 1.
    \begin{equation*} A = 2\ell w + 2 \ell h + 2wh \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} A - 2\ell w = 2 \ell h + 2wh \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} A - 2\ell w = 2h(\ell + w) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} h = \frac{A - 2\ell w}{2(\ell + w)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer 2.
    \begin{equation*} A = 2\ell w + 2 \ell h + 2wh \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} A - 2\ell w = 2 \ell h + 2wh \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} A - 2\ell w = h(2\ell + 2w) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} h = \frac{A - 2\ell w}{2\ell + 2w} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Both answers to the previous example are correct. The first answer factored out a \(2\) as well as \(h\text{,}\) then divided both sides by \(2(\ell + w)\text{.}\) The second answer only factored out \(h\text{,}\) then divided both sides by \(2\ell + 2w\text{.}\) Both answers correctly solved for \(h\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 1.8.57.

    Solve for \(F\) in the equation for kinetic energy: \(E = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 + F d\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    First, notice that the variable \(F\) appears in one of the two terms on the right side. (The focus here is on the word term, recalling that terms are separated by plus or minus signs.) We subtract the term other term \(\frac12 m v^2\) from both sides to isolate the term with \(F\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} E = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 + F d \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} E - \frac{1}{2} m v^2 = F d \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} F = \frac{E - \frac{1}{2} m v^2}{d} \end{equation*}
    From the starting equation \(E = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 + F d\) it is tempting to start by dividing both sides by \(d\text{.}\) However, doing this leads to \(\frac{E}{d} = \frac{\frac12 m v^2 + Fd}{d}\) and we cannot cancel \(d\) on top with \(d\) on bottom since the \(d\) on top is not a factor of the entire numerator.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.8.58.

    Solve for \(h\) in the formula for the volume of a cylinder: \(V = \pi r^2 h\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} V = \pi r^2 h \end{equation*}
    Note that on the right side, \(\pi\) and \(r^2\) and \(h\) are all factors. So, if we were to divide by something on both sides, cancellations would be possible (recall that by divding on both sides, we’ll create a fraction, and in the fraction we create, only factors will cancel: fortunately, what we see on the right side -- which will eventually be the numerator of our fraction -- consists of factors!)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{V}{\pi r^2} = \frac{\pi r^2 h}{\pi r^2} \end{equation*}
    The fraction on the right side has \(\pi\) as a factor on top and bottom, so these cancel. The fraction on the right side also has \(r^2\) as a factor on top and bottom, so these cancel.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{V}{\pi r^2} = h \end{equation*}

    Subsection Equations with Rational Expressions

    We previously simplified rational expressions, so now let’s solve equations that have rational expressions. Recall that a rational expression is a fraction where the numerator and denominator are both polynomials. When solving equations with rational expressions, there are two key ideas from the past to keep in mind:
    1. Strategy 1.4.1 still applies: it is always fastest to solve an equation with fractions by first computing the least common multiple of all denominators, then multiplying both sides of the equation by this expression to eliminate all fractions.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Principle 1.4.13 still applies: if a variable appears in the denominator, we need to check our solutions to make sure that we are not getting any values that would cause any denominator equal to zero.
      🔗
      🔗
    As long as we keep these two ideas from the past in mind, we solve these new types of equations using the same techniques as we have seen for other equations: namely, we apply the same action to both sides.
    🔗

    Example 1.8.59.

    Solve \(\frac{2x + 3}{x - 1} = 4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    The only denominator in this equation is \(x-1\) since we have only one fraction so we multiply both sides by \(x-1\) to eliminate the fraction:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2x + 3}{x - 1} (x-1) = 4(x-1) \end{equation*}
    On the left side, the \(x-1\) cancels, leaving:
    \begin{equation*} 2x + 3 = 4(x-1) \end{equation*}
    We now have an equation without fractions, so we can proceed as usual:
    \begin{equation*} 2x + 3 = 4x - 4 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 3 + 4 = 4x - 2x \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 7 = 2x \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = \frac72 \end{equation*}
    Finally, we need to check that our solution does not cause any denominator to be zero. The only denominator in the original equation was \(x-1\text{,}\) and substituting in our solution gives \(\frac72 - 1 = \frac52\text{,}\) which is not zero. Therefore, our solution of \(x = \frac72\) is valid.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.8.60.

    Solve \(\frac{3}{x+2} + \frac{4}{x-1} = \frac{6}{x^2 + x - 2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    The denominators in this equation are \(x+2\text{,}\) \(x-1\text{,}\) and \(x^2 + x - 2\text{.}\) Factoring the last denominator gives \((x+2)(x-1)\text{,}\) so the least common multiple of all denominators is \((x+2)(x-1)\text{.}\) We multiply both sides by \((x+2)(x-1)\) to eliminate all fractions:
    \begin{equation*} (x+2)(x-1) \left(\frac{3}{x+2} + \frac{4}{x-1}\right)= \frac{6}{(x+2)(x-1)} (x+2)(x-1) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (x+2)(x-1) \frac{3}{x+2} + (x+2)(x-1) \frac{4}{x-1}= \frac{6}{(x+2)(x-1)} (x+2)(x-1) \end{equation*}
    On the left side, the \(x+2\) cancels in the first term and the \(x-1\) cancels in the second term, leaving:
    \begin{equation*} 3(x-1) + 4(x+2) = 6 \end{equation*}
    We now have an equation without fractions, so we can proceed as usual:
    \begin{equation*} 3x - 3 + 4x + 8 = 6 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 7x + 5 = 6 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 7x = 1 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = \frac17 \end{equation*}
    Finally, we need to check that our solution does not cause any denominator to be zero. The denominators in the original equation were \(x+2\text{,}\) \(x-1\text{,}\) and \((x+2)(x-1)\text{.}\) Substituting in our solution gives:
    \begin{equation*} \frac17 + 2 = \frac{15}{7} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \frac17 - 1 = \frac{-6}{7} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac17 + 2\right)\left(\frac17 - 1\right) = \frac{15}{7} \cdot \frac{-6}{7} = \frac{-90}{49} \end{equation*}
    None of these three denominators are zero, so our solution of \(x = \frac17\) is valid.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.8.61.

    Solve \(\frac{2}{x-2} + \frac{4}{x+3} = \frac{x+1}{x^2 + x - 6}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    The denominators in this equation are \(x-2\text{,}\) \(x+3\text{,}\) and \(x^2 + x - 6\text{.}\) Factoring the last denominator gives \((x-2)(x+3)\text{,}\) so the least common multiple of all denominators is \((x-2)(x+3)\text{.}\) We multiply both sides by \((x-2)(x+3)\) to eliminate all fractions:
    \begin{equation*} (x-2)(x+3) \left(\frac{2}{x-2} + \frac{4}{x+3}\right)= \frac{x+1}{(x-2)(x+3)} (x-2)(x+3) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (x-2)(x+3) \frac{2}{x-2} + (x-2)(x+3) \frac{4}{x+3}= \frac{x+1}{(x-2)(x+3)} (x-2)(x+3) \end{equation*}
    On the left side, the \(x-2\) cancels in the first term and the \(x+3\) cancels in the second term, leaving:
    \begin{equation*} 2(x+3) + 4(x-2) = x + 1 \end{equation*}
    We now have an equation without fractions, so we can proceed as usual:
    \begin{equation*} 2x + 6 + 4x - 8 = x + 1 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 6x - 2 = x + 1 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 5x - 2 = 1 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 5x = 3 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = \frac35 \end{equation*}
    Finally, we need to check that our solution does not cause any denominator to be zero. The denominators in the original equation were \(x-2\text{,}\) \(x+3\text{,}\) and \((x-2)(x+3)\text{.}\) Substituting in our solution gives:
    \begin{equation*} \frac35 - 2 = \frac{-7}{5} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \frac35 + 3 = \frac{18}{5} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac35 - 2\right)\left(\frac35 + 3\right) = \frac{-7}{5} \cdot \frac{18}{5} = \frac{-126}{25} \end{equation*}
    None of these three denominators are zero, so our solution of \(x = \frac35\) is valid.

    Subsection Applications revisited

    Example 1.8.62.

    You need \(12,500\) miles for a flight. Your credit card usually transfers miles at a \(1:1\) ratio, but is currently offering a \(10\%\) bonus on all transfers. That is, when you transfer \(1,000\) miles to the airline, instead of getting the usual \(1,000\) miles, you will get \(1,100\) miles because of the \(10\%\) bonus. How many miles should you transfer to book the flight while taking advantage of the transfer bonus?
    🔗
    Answer.
    Let \(x\) be the number of miles you should transfer. Then, because of the \(10\%\) bonus, you will receive \(x + 0.10x\) miles. We want this to equal \(12,500\) miles:
    \begin{equation*} x + 0.10x = 12500 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (1+0.10)x= 12500 \end{equation*}
    Dividing both sides by \(1 + 0.10\) gives:
    \begin{equation*} x = \frac{12,500}{1+0.10} = 11,363.64 \end{equation*}
    So you should transfer approximately \(11,364\) miles to book the flight.

    Example 1.8.63.

    You have a scholarship which allocates spending \(1500\) dollars on a laptop, including the tax. Assuming that tax is \(4\%\text{,}\) what is the pre-tax price of the laptop you can afford using this scholarship?
    🔗
    Answer.
    Let \(p\) be the pre-tax price of the laptop. Then, including tax, the total price of the laptop is \(p + 0.04p\text{.}\) We want this to equal \(1500\) dollars:
    \begin{equation*} p + 0.04p = 1500 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (1 + 0.04)p = 1500 \end{equation*}
    Dividing both sides by \(1 + 0.04\) gives:
    \begin{equation*} p = \frac{1500}{1 + 0.04} = 1442.31 \end{equation*}
    So the pre-tax price of the laptop you can afford is approximately \(1442.31\) dollars.

    Example 1.8.64.

    Alice leaves from City X to head to City Y starting at noon traveling at \(50\) miles per hour. City X and City Y are \(200\) miles apart. Beth leaves from City Y to head to City X at \(30\) minutes past noon traveling at \(60\) miles per hour. How much time does Beth drive for?
    🔗
    Answer 1.
    Let’s let \(t\) represent the amount of hours that Alice drives. Since Alice drives at \(50\) miles per hour for \(t\) hours, the distance she drives is \(50t\text{.}\) Since Beth drives at \(60\) miles per hour, and she starts \(0.5\) hours after Alice, she drives for \(t - 0.5\) hours, so the distance she drives is \(60(t - 0.5)\text{.}\) Since the two cities are \(200\) miles apart, we have:
    \begin{equation*} 50t + 60(t - 0.5) = 200 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 50t + 60t - 30 = 200 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 110t = 230 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} t = \frac{230}{110} = 2.09 \end{equation*}
    So Alice drives for approximately \(2.09\) hours. Since Beth starts \(0.5\) hours after Alice, Beth drives for approximately \(2.09 - 0.5 = 1.59\) hours.
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer 2.
    Let’s let \(s\) represent the amount of hours that Beth drives. Since Beth drives at \(60\) miles per hour for \(s\) hours, the distance she drives is \(60s\text{.}\) Since Alice drives at \(50\) miles per hour, and she starts \(0.5\) hours before Beth, she drives for \(s + 0.5\) hours, so the distance she drives is \(50(s + 0.5)\text{.}\) Since the two cities are \(200\) miles apart, we have:
    \begin{equation*} 50(s + 0.5) + 60s = 200 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 50s + 25 + 60s = 200 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 110s = 175 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} s = \frac{175}{110} = 1.59 \end{equation*}
    So Beth drives for approximately \(1.59\) hours.
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer 3.
    Let’s let \(t\) represent the amount of hours that Alice drives, and \(s\) represent the amount of hours that Beth drives. Since Alice drives at \(50\) miles per hour for \(t\) hours, the distance she drives is \(50t\text{.}\) Since Beth drives at \(60\) miles per hour for \(s\) hours, the distance she drives is \(60s\text{.}\) Since the two cities are \(200\) miles apart, we have:
    \begin{equation*} 50t + 60s = 200 \end{equation*}
    Additionally, since Beth starts \(0.5\) hours after Alice, we have:
    \begin{equation*} s = t - 0.5 \end{equation*}
    Substituting this into the first equation gives:
    \begin{equation*} 50t + 60(t - 0.5) = 200 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 50t + 60t - 30 = 200 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 110t = 230 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} t = \frac{230}{110} = 2.09 \end{equation*}
    So Alice drives for approximately \(2.09\) hours. Since Beth starts \(0.5\) hours after Alice, Beth drives for approximately \(2.09 - 0.5 = 1.59\) hours.

    Example 1.8.65.

    The CDC recommends hand sanitizer that’s at least \(60\%\) alcohol to deactivate the virus that causes COVID-19, but stores are out of stock due to overpurchasing. You have a \(32\) fl oz bottle of \(91\%\) isopropyl alcohol at home. It’s a good thing you bought this years ago, but since its strength is more than enough, you don’t want to waste it. How much lotion (with no alcohol in it) should you mix in to create a homemade hand sanitizer that’s \(60\%\) alcohol?
    🔗
    Answer.
    Let \(x\) be the amount of lotion (in fl oz) to mix in. (Note that the lotion has no alcohol: in other words, the concentration of alcohol in the lotion is \(0\%\text{.}\)) The first container (the bottle of isopropyl alcohol) has \(32\) fl oz of liquid, with a concentration of \(91\%\) which can be written \(0.91\text{,}\) so the amount of alcohol is \(0.91 \cdot 32\) fl oz. The second container (the lotion) has \(x\) fl oz of liquid, with a concentration of \(0\%\) which can be written \(0.00\text{,}\) so the amount of alcohol is \(0.00 \cdot x = 0\) fl oz.
    🔗
    After mixing, the total amount of liquid is \(32 + x\) fl oz, and we want the concentration of alcohol to be \(60\%\) which can be written \(0.60\text{.}\) Therefore, the amount of alcohol after mixing should be \(0.60(32 + x)\) fl oz.
    🔗
    Also, we know that the first container is contributing \(0.91 \cdot 32\) fl oz of alcohol, and the second container is contributing \(0\) fl oz of alcohol, so the total amount of alcohol after mixing is:
    \begin{equation*} 0.91 \cdot 32 + 0 = 0.62(32+x) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 0.91(32) = 0.62(32) + 0.62x \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 0.91(32) - 0.62(32) = 0.62x \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \frac{0.91(32) - 0.62(32)}{0.62} = x \end{equation*}
    Calculating the right side gives approximately \(15.16\text{.}\) So you should mix in approximately \(15.16\) fl oz of lotion.
    🔗

    Subsection Summary

    1. When writing expressions involving percentages, always ask what we are taking a percentage of. (For example, if we are taking \(20\%\) of the original price, and \(x\) represents the original price, then we write \(0.20x\text{.}\))
      🔗
      🔗
    2. An expression such as \(0.20x\) may then need to be adjusted depending on the context. For example \(x + 0.20x\) represents the original price plus a \(20\%\) increase (which would apply when paying more, such as a tax), while \(x - 0.20x\) represents the original price minus a \(20\%\) decrease (which would apply when paying less, such as getting a discount).
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Apply the distance formula to each trip in a distance problem.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Apply the concentration formula to each container in a mixture problem.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. To solve literal equations, apply the usual technique of solving equations (performing the same action on both sides of an equation), but without simplifying expressions after each step.
      🔗
      🔗
    6. To solve equations with rational expressions, first multiply both sides by the least common multiple of all denominators to eliminate all fractions, then proceed to solve the resulting equation as usual. If a variable appears in the denominator, check that your solutions do not cause any denominator in the original equation to be zero.
      🔗
