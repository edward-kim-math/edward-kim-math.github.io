Example 1.4.5.

Solve the equation \(\frac{x}{6} + \frac{1}{2} = \frac{2}{3}\text{.}\)
🔗
Solution.
The least common multiple of the denominators \(6\) and \(2\) and \(3\) is \(6\text{.}\) So we multiply both sides of the equation by \(6\text{,}\) though Order of Operations requires us to wrap the left side in parentheses:
\begin{equation*} 6 \left( \frac{x}{6} + \frac{1}{2} \right) = 6 \cdot \frac{2}{3}. \end{equation*}
Distributing on the left side gives:
\begin{equation*} 6 \cdot \frac{x}{6} + 6 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 6 \cdot \frac{2}{3}. \end{equation*}
Now we cancel where applicable.
\begin{equation*} \cancel{6} \cdot 1 \cdot \frac{x}{\cancel{6}} + \cancel{2} \cdot 3 \cdot \frac{1}{\cancel{2}} = \cancel{3} \cdot 2 \cdot \frac{2}{\cancel{3}}. \end{equation*}
After performing the cancellations, we are left with:
\begin{equation*} x + 9 = 12. \end{equation*}
This is an equation with no fractions. Subtracting \(9\) from both sides gives:
\begin{equation*} x = 3. \end{equation*}
