Example 1.4.6.

Solve \(\frac{x}{6} + \frac{x-3}{10} = \frac43\)
🔗
Solution 1.
Here’s what it looks like to solve \(\frac{x}{6} + \frac{x-3}{10} = \frac{4}{3}\) by ignoring Strategy 1.4.1 and doing everything with common denominators instead:
\begin{equation*} \frac{x}{6} + \frac{x-3}{10} = \frac{4}{3} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \frac{5x}{30} + \frac{3(x-3)}{30} = \frac{40}{30} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \frac{5x}{30} + \frac{3x-9}{30} = \frac{40}{30} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \frac{5x + 3x - 9}{30} = \frac{40}{30} \end{equation*}
Multiply both sides by \(30\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} 5x + 3x - 9 = 40 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 8x - 9 = 40 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 8x = 49 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} x = \frac{49}{8} \end{equation*}

Solution 2.
Here’s what it looks like to solve \(\frac{x}{6} + \frac{x-3}{10} = \frac{4}{3}\) by using Strategy 1.4.1. First, the least common multiple of the denominators \(6\) and \(10\) and \(3\) is \(30\text{,}\) so we make our first step to multiply both sides by \(30\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} 30\left(\frac{x}{6} + \frac{x-3}{10}\right) = 30 \cdot \frac{4}{3} \end{equation*}
On the left side, distribute \(30\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} 30 \frac{x}{6} + 30 \frac{x-3}{10} = 30 \cdot \frac{4}{3} \end{equation*}
After cancelling, we have:
\begin{equation*} 5x + 3(x-3) = 10 \cdot 4 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 5x + 3x - 9 = 40 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 8x - 9 = 40 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 8x = 49 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} x = \frac{49}{8} \end{equation*}
