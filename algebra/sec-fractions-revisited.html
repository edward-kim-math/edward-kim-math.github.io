    Section 1.7 Fractions Revisited

    Objectives

    In this section, we learn how to:
    🔗
    1. Add, subtract, multiply, and divide rational expressions.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Simplify complex fractions.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    This section extends our previous work with fractions that have more involved expressions containing variables in the numerator and denominator. We will practice the techniques of adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing fractions that we learned in Section 1.2 in this new context. We will combine this with the factoring techniques we learned in Section 1.6. In the end, this section is meant to have no surprises: it is a good opportunity to revisit concepts to have seen, though portions of each problem will feel new, because we are combining concepts from two previous sections in ways that we never have been able to yet.
    🔗
    Hang in there. You’ve been asked to take in a lot of mathematics. If you feel overwhelmed or frustrated, take a break. Circle back to content to dig into something works. When you can practice after having actively thought about why a certain technique works, this will free your mind from having to work only from memorization. This will free up some capacity to take in new stuff. It will take time, and it’s important for you to be patient with yourself!
    🔗

    Subsection Rational Expressions

    When we saw fractions that had constants in the numerator and denominator, we could rewrite a fraction by multiplying by the same value on top and on bottom, or cancel a factor (it had to be a factor, because we’re undoing multiplication) that appeared on both top and bottom. For example, \(\frac{15}{18} = \frac{5 \cdot 3}{6 \cdot 3} = \frac{5}{6}\) by cancelling the common factor of \(3\text{.}\) The work above explicitly showed \(\frac{5 \cdot 3}{6 \cdot 3}\text{,}\) but we could have written \(\frac{15}{18}=\frac{5}{6}\) directly. We apply the same concept (canceling common factors) when variables appear in the numerator and denominator.
    🔗

    Example 1.7.1.

    Simplify \(\frac{x-5}{x^2-25}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    As written, we cannot cancel anything (because we can only cancel common factors). We need factors, and recall from Principle 1.1.74 that factoring produces factors. As a first step, we will factor the denominator.
    🔗
    \(\frac{x-5}{x^2-25} = \frac{x-5}{(x-5)(x+5)} = \frac{\cancel{(x-5)}}{\cancel{(x-5)}(x+5)} = \frac{1}{x+5}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    To clarify why we cannot cancel in the initial fraction \(\frac{x-5}{x^2-25}\text{,}\) the \(x\) on top does not cancel with one of the \(x\)s on bottom, and neither does the \(5\) on top partially with the \(25\) on bottom: recall from Warning 1.2.26 that we can only cancel factors, not terms.
    🔗

    Example 1.7.2.

    Simplify \(\frac{x^2 - 11x + 24}{x^2 - 13x + 30}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    One potential temptation is to try to “cancel” the \(x^2\) appearing on top and bottom, but these are not factors, so they cannot cancel. So, as our first step, we will factor in the numerator and factor in the denominator. \(\frac{x^2 - 11x + 24}{x^2 - 13x + 30} = \frac{(x-3)(x-8)}{(x-3)(x-10)} = \frac{x-8}{x-10}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In our final answer \(\frac{x-8}{x-10}\) we could have kept parentheses around the numerator and parentheses around the denominator, but we chose to drop both sets for simplicity: recall from Principle 1.2.83 that the top of every fraction is in a hidden set of parentheses and the bottom of every fraction is in a hidden set of parentheses.
    🔗
    We could not simplify any further than \(\frac{x-8}{x-10}\text{.}\) The \(x\) on top and bottom are not factors. In addition, even if we wrote \(8 = 4 \cdot 2\) and \(10 = 5 \cdot 2\) in
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x- 4 \cdot 2}{x - 5 \cdot 2}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    we still could not cancel the \(2\)s because they are not factors of the numerator and denominator. (In Note 1.1.39 we noted that \(2\) is not a factor of \(x - 4 \cdot 2\text{.}\))
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The formulas for simplifying addition and subtraction of fractions in Principle 1.2.31 and Principle 1.2.41 required getting a common denominator. This remains true when fractions contain variables.
    🔗

    Example 1.7.3.

    Simplify \(\frac{4}{(5s-2)^2}+\frac{s}{5s-2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(\frac{4}{(5s-2)^2}+\frac{s}{5s-2} = \frac{4}{(5s-2)(5s-2)}+\frac{s(5s-2)}{(5s-2)(5s-2)} = \frac{4 + s(5s-2)}{(5s-2)^2} = \frac{4 + 5s^2 - 2s}{(5s-2)^2}\)
    🔗
    Since no additional canceling is possible, the denominator could also be written \((5s-2)(5s-2)\) or \(25S^2 - 20s + 4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.7.4.

    Simplify \(\frac{4x+12}{x^2+6x+9}+\frac{5x}{x^2-9}+\frac{7}{x-3}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(\frac{4x+12}{x^2+6x+9}+\frac{5x}{x^2-9}+\frac{7}{x-3} = \frac{4(x+3)}{(x+3)^2}+\frac{5x}{(x+3)(x-3)}+\frac{7}{x-3} = \frac{4}{x+3}+\frac{5x}{(x+3)(x-3)}+\frac{7}{x-3} = \frac{4(x-3)}{(x+3)(x-3)}+\frac{5x}{(x+3)(x-3)}+\frac{7(x+3)}{(x+3)(x-3)} = \frac{4x-12+5x+7x+21}{(x+3)(x-3)} = \frac{16x+9}{(x+3)(x-3)}\)
    🔗
    Since no additional canceling is possible, the denominator could also be written \(x^2-9\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.7.5.

    Simplify \(\frac{t}{t+3}+\frac{4t}{t-3}-\frac{18}{t^2-9}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(\frac{t}{t+3}+\frac{4t}{t-3}-\frac{18}{t^2-9} = \frac{t}{t+3}\cdot\frac{t-3}{t-3}+\frac{4t}{t-3}\cdot\frac{t+3}{t+3}-\frac{18}{t^2-9} = \frac{t(t-3)}{t^2-9}+\frac{4t(t+3)}{t^2-9}-\frac{18}{t^2-9} = \frac{t(t-3)+4t(t+3)-18}{t^2-9} = \frac{t^2-3t+4t^2+12t-18}{t^2-9} = \frac{5t^2+9t-18}{t^2-9}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    When multiplying two fractions, Principle 1.2.56 tells us to just multiply straight across. We still do this even when there are variables in the fractions
    🔗

    Example 1.7.6.

    Simplify \(\frac{(x+2)}{(2x-3)} \cdot \frac{(2x^2-3x)}{(x^2-4)}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(\frac{(x+2)}{(2x-3)} \cdot \frac{(2x^2-3x)}{(x^2-4)} = \frac{(x+2)(2x^2-3x)}{(2x-3)(x^2-4)} = \frac{(x+2)x(2x-3)}{(2x-3)(x-2)(x+2)} = \frac{\cancel{(x+2)}x\cancel{(2x-3)}}{\cancel{(2x-3)}(x-2)\cancel{(x+2)}} = \frac{x}{x-2}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Recall that to divide two fractions, Principle 1.2.67 tells us to multiply by the reciprocal of the second fraction.
    🔗

    Example 1.7.7.

    Simplify \(\frac{4x^2-9}{2x^2+7x+6} \div \frac{27x^4+8x}{9x^4-6x^3+4x^2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(\frac{4x^2-9}{2x^2+7x+6} \div \frac{27x^4+8x}{9x^4-6x^3+4x^2} = \frac{4x^2-9}{2x^2+7x+6} \cdot \frac{9x^4-6x^3+4x^2}{27x^4+8x} = \frac{(4x^2-9)(9x^4-6x^3+4x^2)}{(2x^2+7x+6)(27x^4+8x)} = \frac{(2x-3)(2x+3)x^2(9x^2-6x+4)}{(2x^2+7x+6)x(27x^3+8)} = \frac{(2x-3)(2x+3)x^2(9x^2-6x+4)}{(2x+3)(x+2)x(3x+2)(9x^2-6x+4)} = \frac{(2x-3)\cancel{(2x+3)}x^{\cancelto{1}{2}}\cancel{(9x^2-6x+4)}}{\cancel{(2x+3)}(x+2)\cancel{x}(3x+2)\cancel{(9x^2-6x+4)}} = \frac{(2x-3)x}{(x+2)(3x+2)}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Compound Fractions

    Principle 1.2.67 tells us that to do one fraction divided by another fraction, we can take the first fraction and instead multiply it by the reciprocal of the second fraction. The application of reciprocals will be useful in all situations where we have a compound fraction, which is a fraction where the numerator, the denominator, or both are themselves contain fractions.
    🔗

    Strategy 1.7.8. Simplifying a fraction with fractions inside.

    • When does this strategy apply? This strategy applies any time we need to simplify a compound fraction, which is a fraction where the numerator and/or the denominator contain fractions.
      🔗
      🔗
    • How to apply the strategy
      1. Independently simplify the numerator into a single fraction and simplify the denominator into a single fraction.
        🔗
        🔗
      2. Once both the numerator and denominator are single fractions, rewrite fraction divided by fraction instead as fraction multiplied by the reciprocal of the second fraction.
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    “In a moment, we will be asked to simplify \(\frac{\frac{3}{x+2} + \frac{x}{x+2}}{\frac{5x}{x^2-4} + \frac{3}{x-2}}\text{,}\) and we could reinterpret this expression as
    \begin{equation*} \left( \frac{3}{x+2} + \frac{x}{x+2} \right) \div \left( \frac{5x}{x^2-4} + \frac{3}{x-2} \right) \end{equation*}
    where the parentheses are required (due to order of operations, and recalling that the entire numerator and entire denominator of a fraction are both in a hidden sets of parentheses). While we need the reciprocal of the denominator, the problem is the denominator \(\frac{5x}{x^2-4} + \frac{3}{x-2}\) is not a single fraction. It is incorrect to say that the reciprocal is \(\frac{x^2-4}{5x} + \frac{x-2}{3}\text{.}\) That is, we cannot just reciprocate each term separately. We can only reciprocate a single fraction, which is why Strategy 1.7.8 tells us to first simplify the numerator into a single fraction and the denominator into a single fraction. This is exactly what we will do as we present the full example:”

    Example 1.7.9.

    Simplify \(\frac{\frac{3}{x+2} + \frac{x}{x+2}}{\frac{5x}{x^2-4} + \frac{1}{x-2}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    First, simplify the numerator and denominator separately. The numerator is \(\frac{3}{x+2} + \frac{x}{x+2}\text{,}\) which can be combined since they have the same denominator: \(\frac{3 + x}{x+2}\text{.}\) Independently, we will simplify the denominator. (By independent, we mean that none of the work in the numerator and none of the work in the denominator influence each other.) The denominator is \(\frac{5x}{x^2-4} + \frac{3}{x-2}\text{.}\) Note that \(x^2 - 4 = (x-2)(x+2)\text{,}\) so the common denominator is \((x-2)(x+2)\text{.}\) Rewrite each term with this common denominator: \(\frac{5x}{(x-2)(x+2)} + \frac{3(x+2)}{(x-2)(x+2)}\text{.}\) Combine the numerators: \(\frac{5x + 3(x+2)}{(x-2)(x+2)} = \frac{5x + 3x + 6}{(x-2)(x+2)} = \frac{8x + 6}{(x-2)(x+2)}\text{.}\) Now, the original expression is \(\frac{\frac{3 + x}{x+2}}{\frac{8x + 6}{(x-2)(x+2)}}\text{.}\) Rewrite as multiplication by the reciprocal: \(\frac{3 + x}{x+2} \cdot \frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{8x + 6}\text{.}\) Cancel the common factor \(x+2\text{:}\) \(\frac{3 + x}{\cancel{x+2}} \cdot \frac{(x-2)\cancel{(x+2)}}{8x + 6} = \frac{3 + x}{1} \cdot \frac{x-2}{8x + 6}\text{.}\) Simplify the numerator of the second fraction: \(8x + 6 = 2(4x + 3)\text{.}\) So the expression becomes \((3 + x) \cdot \frac{x-2}{2(4x + 3)}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.7.10.

    Simplify \(\frac{\frac{r}{s}+\frac{s}{r}}{\frac{r^2}{s^2}-\frac{s^2}{r^2}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \begin{align*} \frac{\frac{r}{s}+\frac{s}{r}}{\frac{r^2}{s^2}-\frac{s^2}{r^2}} \amp= \frac{ \frac{r^2}{rs} + \frac{s^2}{rs}}{ \frac{r^4}{s^2 r^2} - \frac{s^4}{r^2 s^2}}\\ \amp= \frac{ \frac{r^2+s^2}{rs}}{ \frac{r^4-s^4}{r^2 s^2}}\\ \amp= \frac{r^2+s^2}{rs} \cdot \frac{r^2 s^2}{r^4 - s^4}\\ \amp= \frac{(r^2+s^2)r^2s^2}{rs(r^4-s^4)}\\ \amp= \frac{(r^2+s^2)rs}{r^4-s^4}\\ \amp= \frac{(r^2+s^2)rs}{(r^2+s^2)(r^2-s^2)}\\ \amp= \frac{rs}{r^2-s^2} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.7.11.

    Simplify \(\frac{\frac{1}{x+h}-\frac{1}{x}}{h}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(\frac{\frac{1}{x+h}-\frac{1}{x}}{h} = \frac{\frac{x - (x+h)}{(x+h)x}}{h} = \frac{\frac{-h}{(x+h)x}}{h} = \frac{-h}{(x+h)x} \cdot \frac{1}{h} = \frac{-\cancel{h}}{(x+h)x\cancel{h}} = \frac{-1}{(x+h)x}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Summary

    1. We can take a fraction (including a fraction with variables in the numerator and/or denominator) and create a fraction of the same value by multiplying by the same quantity on the top and bottom, or undo this as well (we can cancel common factors on top and bottom, though we may need to factor first).
      🔗
      🔗
    2. To simplify adding or subtracting fractions (including a fraction with variables in the numerator and/or denominator), we need a common denominator first.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. To multiply fractions (including a fraction with variables in the numerator and/or denominator), multiply straight across whether we have a common denominator or not.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. To divide two fractions (including a fraction with variables in the numerator and/or denominator), note that dividing by a fraction is the same thing as multiplying by its reciprocal.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. When fractions appear in the numerator or denominator of a larger fraction, we can simplify by multiplying by the reciprocal, but we first need to turn the top and bottom into single fractions first.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
