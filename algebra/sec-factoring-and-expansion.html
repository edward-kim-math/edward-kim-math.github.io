    In this section, we will look at products of binomials, which are expressions of the format \((a+b)(c+d)\text{.}\) As before, a product means the result of multiplication, and that multiplication happens between the value of \((a+b)\) and the vaule of \((c+d)\text{.}\) By binomial, we mean \((a+b)\) is a binomial, and \((c+d)\) is a binomial. We start with expansion, where we turn this product of binomials \((a+b)(c+d)\) into an expression of the format \(Ax^2+Bx+C\text{,}\) known as a trinomial. Because the highest power is a \(2\text{,}\) we note that \(Ax^2+Bx+C\) is a quadratic. In \(Ax^2+Bx+C\text{,}\) we will call \(A\) the quadratic coefficient, call \(B\) the linear coefficient, and call \(C\) the constant term. For example, in \(5x^2+6x+7\text{,}\) the quadratic coefficient is \(5\text{,}\) the linear coefficient is \(6\text{,}\) and the constant term is \(7\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We will start with \((a+b)(c+d)\) where both \(a\) and \(c\) are the variable \(x\text{.}\) This will lead to the quadratic coefficient \(A\) in \(Ax^2+Bx+C\) always being \(1\text{.}\) In these all such examples, we will start with expressions of the form \((a+b)(c+d)\text{,}\) but then look at the expansion \(Ax^2+Bx+C\text{,}\) comparing the expansions from several examples to see if there are any patterns. Spoiler alert: some of the patterns can be come about from examining which coefficients in \(Ax^2+Bx+C\) are positive and which coefficients are negative.
    🔗
    Let’s take our initial Distributive Law \(a(b+c) = ab + ac\text{,}\) for the sake of clarity, temporarily replace the letters \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\) with \(x\) and \(y\) and \(z\) instead. It is technically the same formula to have
    \begin{equation*} x(y+z) = xy + xz \end{equation*}
    but in this version of the Distributive Law, we will subsitute \(x=a+b\) and \(y=c\) and \(z=d\) to get
    \begin{equation*} (a+b)(c+d) = (a+b)c + (a+b)d. \end{equation*}
    Note that in the equation above, the left side is \((a+b)(c+d)\) instead of \(a+b(c+d)\) due to the Order of Operations: we really needed the value of \(x\text{,}\) which is \(a+b\text{,}\) to be multiplied by \((c+d)\text{.}\) Due to the fact that the Order of Operations says to do multiplication before addition, we need to use parentheses to protect the value of \(a+b\text{,}\) since it is the value of \(a+b\) that needs to be multiplied by \(c+d\text{.}\)
    🔗
    On the right side of the equation above, we have \((a+b)c + (a+b)d\text{,}\) and we can apply the Distributive Law to rewrite \((a+b)c\) as \(ac + bc\text{,}\) and rewrite \((a+b)d\) as \(ad + bd\text{.}\) Putting all the pieces together, we have
    \begin{equation*} (a+b)(c+d) = ac + bc + ad + bd. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Principle 1.6.1. Expanding the Product of Two Binomials.

    \begin{equation*} (a+b)(c+d) = ac + ad + bc + bd \end{equation*}

    🔗

    Example 1.6.2.

    Expand and simplify \((x+3)(x+5)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    We can use Principle 1.6.1 with \(a=x\) and \(b=3\) and \(c=x\) and \(d=5\) to get
    \begin{equation*} (x+3)(x+5) = x\cdot x + x\cdot 5 + 3\cdot x + 3\cdot 5 = x^2 + 5x + 3x + 15 = x^2 + 8x + 15. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Note 1.6.3.

    Principle 1.6.14.

    \begin{equation*} (a-b)(c-d) = ac - ad - bc + bd \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Whether you choose to use this version \((a-b)(c-d) = ac - ad - bc + bd\) directly or think via Principle 1.6.1 is a matter of personal choice. Let’s demonstrate the two ways to think about this through an example.
    🔗

    Example 1.6.15.

    Expand and simplify \((x-3)(x-8)\)
    🔗
    Answer 1.
    The given expression \((x-3)(x-8)\) fits the format of the left side \((a-b)(c-d)\) in Principle 1.6.14 with \(a=x\) and \(b=3\) and \(c=x\) and \(d=8\text{.}\) In doing these identifications, the confusion may be why the stating of \(b\) and \(d\) do not have minus signs, but this is because the format of \((a-b)(c-d)\) already includes minus signs. Then, by Principle 1.6.14,
    \begin{equation*} (x-3)(x-8) = x \cdot x - x \cdot 8 - 3 \cdot x + 3 \cdot 8 = x^2 - 8x - 3x + 24 = x^2 - 11x + 24. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer 2.
    We can rewrite the expression \((x-3)(x-8) = (x+(-3))(x+(-8))\) because \((x+(-3))(x+(-8))\) fits the format of the left side \((a+b)(c+d)\) in Principle 1.6.1. In this format, \(a=x\text{,}\) and \(b\) should consist of content right after the first plus sign, so \(b=-3\text{.}\) Similarly, \(c=x\text{,}\) and \(d\) should be all the content after the second plus sign, so \(d=-8\text{.}\) Then by Principle 1.6.1,
    \begin{equation*} (x+(-3))(x+(-8)) = x \cdot x + x \cdot (-8) + (-3) \cdot x + (-3)(-8) = x^2 - 8x - 3x + 24 = x^2-11x+24. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Note 1.6.16.

    Because it will be very useful later, we examine the final expression \(x^2-11x+24\) identifying that it is in the form \(Ax^2+Bx+C\) will need us to first slightly rewrite the final expression as \(x^2 + (-11)x + 24\text{.}\) by noting the quadratic coefficient is \(A=1\text{,}\) the linear coefficient \(B=-11\text{,}\) and the constant term is \(C=24\text{.}\) It is very important to note that the linear coefficient \(-11\) is the sum of the numbers \(-3\) and \(-8\) in the original expression, while the constant term \(24\) is the product of the numbers \(-3\) and \(-8\) in the original expression. (While we noted \((-3)+(-8)=-11\) we can also note \(3+8=11\text{.}\))
    🔗
    🔗

You may prefer to apply principles such as a negative times a negative results in a positive, without needing to introduce a variant of Principle 1.6.1.

    Expand and simplify \((x+7)(x-2)\text{.}\)

    Answer.
    We can use the first formula in Principle 1.6.25 with \(a=x\) and \(b=7\) and \(c=x\) and \(d=2\) to get
    \begin{equation*} (x+7)(x-2) = x\cdot x - x\cdot 2 + 7\cdot x - 7\cdot 2 = x^2 - 2x + 7x - 14 = x^2 + 5x - 14. \end{equation*}

    For the first time in this section, we end with a quadratic expression \(x^2 + 5x - 14\) where the constant term is negative. This happened because we multiplied \(+7\) with \(-2\text{.}\) Recall the product of a real numbers with opposite signs results in a negative number. In the original expression, let’s examine the \(7\) and the \(2\) which appear after the plus sign and the minus sign. The difference between these numbers is \(5\text{,}\) and note the linear coefficient was \(5\text{.}\)

    🔗

    Subsection Factoring

    Our goal here is to take a trinomial in the format of \(Ax^2+Bx+C\) such as \(x^2+7x-30\) and \(x^2+14x+40\) and factor this, producing a product of binomials. There are various techniques, and it is advisable to understand every techinque introduced, not just one technique. We will point out the connections between the techniques: that is, we point out what questions get asked in various ways but actually require us to do the same thinking. Some of the factoring techniques are ideal in certain situations, while some of the techniques work in more general settings.
    🔗
    We start with the situation where \(A=1\text{.}\) That is, we will focus on factoring a quadratic like \(2x^2+11x-40\) a little later, since the quadratic coefficient \(A=2\text{,}\) and for now instead focus on a quadratic such as \(x^2+7x-30\text{,}\) where the quadratic coefficient \(A=1\text{,}\) seen more clearly when rewritten as \(1x^2+7x-30\text{.}\) We mentioned that some techniques only work in certain situations. To be specific, some of the techniques that we’ll see only apply when \(A=1\text{.}\) Let’s start digging into techniques. It is helpful to see what happens based on whether the constant term is positive or negative.

    Strategy 1.6.40. Factoring a quadratic trinomial into a product of binomials.

    Suppose we are given \(Ax^2+Bx+C\) with \(A=1\text{,}\) meaning we have \(x^2+Bx+C\text{.}\) To factor \(x^2+Bx+C\) as the product of binomials:
    • If the costant term \(C\) is positive, then the signs contained in both binomials match.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If the costant term \(C\) is negative, then the signs contained in both binomials differ.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Now, let’s dig in further to see what happens when the constant term is postive. First, let’s note that when we have the consstant term positive, then the two binomials have to have the same sign, meaning that both signs are positive or both signs are negative. But now, we can see two situations: the linear term can be positive or negative. For example, we can have \(x^2+9x+20\) or we can have \(x^2-9x+20\text{.}\)
    🔗
    It seems like a reasonable guess (and we’ll check if it works) that if we have a \(+9x\) term, that the matching sign in both binomials is a plus, while if we have a \(-9x\) term, then the matching sign in both binomials is a minus.
    🔗
    Let’s dig in to see if we can find the missing values in
    \begin{equation*} (x+\_\_\_)(x+\_\_\_) = x^2+9x+20. \end{equation*}
    To match the notation earlier, let’s fill in these blanks with \(b\) and \(d\text{.}\) That is, let’s find the \(b\) and \(d\) in
    \begin{equation*} (x+b)(x+d)=x^2+9x+20 \end{equation*}
    by expanding the left side:
    \begin{equation*} x^2+dx+bx+bd = x^2+9x+20. \end{equation*}
    Now, the middle two terms \(dx\) and \(bx\) are like terms, and we can “collect like terms” here, though what we’ll really do is apply Note 1.1.89 and factor out \(x\) for the middle two terms:
    \begin{equation*} x^2+(d+b)x+bd = x^2+9x+20. \end{equation*}
    The last term on the left \(bd\) has no \(x\text{,}\) so this must be what matches up with \(20\) on the right. The linear coefficient (the number in front of \(x\)) on the left is \(d+b\) and on the right is \(9\text{.}\) This combination of things tells us what we need. The two numbers we need to find (called \(b\) and \(d\) here) need to multiply to \(20\) and need to add to \(9\text{.}\) Those two numbers are \(4\) and \(5\) so we can factor \(x^2+9x+20\) as either
    \begin{equation*} (x+4)(x+5) \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} (x+5)(x+4). \end{equation*}
    These are essentially the same (just switching the order of the factors), and we can check our factoring by expanding. Earlier, we found the two numbers which multiply to \(20\) and add to \(9\text{.}\) Here is a systematic way that we can do that: list pairs of numbers whose product is \(20\text{:}\)
    • \(1\) and \(20\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(2\) and \(10\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(4\) and \(5\)
      🔗
      🔗
    To be systematic, we started with the smallest whole number as a factor, and kept working our way up. Then, look through the list to see which pair of numbers adds up to \(9\text{.}\) In this case, it happens to be \(4\) and \(5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For \(x^2-9x+20\text{,}\) we’ll need minus signs inside both binomials. That is, our set up looks like:
    \begin{equation*} (x-\_\_\_)(x-\_\_\_) = x^2-9x+20. \end{equation*}
    Now, the minus signs are already drawn in, so we just need to fill in numbers after the minus signs. Based on the \(20\) and the number after the minus sign but before the \(x\) being \(9\text{,}\) we are again looking for two numbers whose product is \(20\) and whose sum is \(9\text{,}\) and these are again \(4\) and \(5\text{,}\) which fill in the two blanks:
    \begin{equation*} (x-4)(x-5) \end{equation*}
    or alternately,
    \begin{equation*} (x-5)(x-4). \end{equation*}
    It is always a little more confusing to describe, which is why we are using the following language: in
    \begin{equation*} (x-\_\_\_)(x-\_\_\_) = x^2-9x+20 \end{equation*}
    we have already written in the minus signs, so we just need to include write after the minus signs the two positive numbers that add to \(9\) and multiply to \(20\text{.}\)

    The recipe we gave in Strategy 1.6.41 is very handy for factoring, but only works when the constant term is positive. Let’s do a short exploration to develop a strategy for factoring when the constant term is negative. In the strategy that we’re about to develop, we will be creating a template in which minus signs and plus signs are already in place.
    • When we expand \((x-7)(x+2)\) we get \(x^2+2x-7x-14 = x^2 - 5x - 14\text{.}\) In the factored version, the numbers after the minus sign and the plus sign are \(7\) and \(2\text{.}\) (Note, by saying the number after the minus sign, we mean to intentionally not include the minus sign itself.) The difference between \(7\) and \(2\) is \(5\text{,}\) which is the number after the first minus sign in \(x^2-5x-14\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • When we expand \((x+7)(x-2)\) we get \(x^2-2x+7x-14 = x^2 + 5x - 14\text{.}\) In the factored version, the numbers after the minus sign and plus sign are \(7\) and \(2\) again, just as they were i nthe previous exploration. The difference between \(7\) and \(2\) is \(5\text{,}\) which is the number after the first plus sign in \(x^2+5x-14\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    In both \(x^2-5x-14\) and \(x^2+5x-14\text{,}\) the minus in front of \(14\) means that the signs in the middle of the factors for the factorization of both of these quadratics must include one minus sign and one plus sign. In both factorizations, we saw the numbers \(7\) and \(2\) appear, and their difference is \(5\text{,}\) which appears to be connected to the linear coefficient of either \(5\) or \(-5\text{,}\) depending on which quadratic expression we look at.
    • In the case of \(x^2-5x-14\text{,}\) this negative linear coefficient \(-5\) in front of \(x\) came from collecting the like terms \(2x-7x\) in \(x^2+2x-7x-14\text{,}\) and for the \(2x-7x\) to occur, the factorization required the \(7\) to appear after the minus sign and the \(2\) to appear after the plus sign. (To say this in the form of a shortcut, because the linear coefficient was negative, we needed more “negative \(x\)” than “positive \(x\)”.)
      🔗
      🔗
    • In the case of \(x^2+5x-14\text{,}\) the positive linear coefficient \(5\) in front of \(x\) came from collecting like terms \(-2x+7x\) in \(x^2-2x+7x-14\text{,}\) which can only occur if the factorization has the \(2\) appearing after the minus sign and the \(7\) appearing before the minus sign. In short, to get the linear coefficient to be positive, we needed more positive than negative when we collect like terms.
      🔗
      🔗

    Subsection Additional factoring techniques

    We now introduce a strategy that often applies when factoring an expression consisting of four terms:
    🔗

    Strategy 1.6.102. Factor by grouping.

    • When does this strategy apply? This strategy usually (but not always) applies when factoring an expression consisting of four terms.
      🔗
      🔗
    • How to apply the strategy
      1. Factor an expression out of the first two terms and factor an expression out of the last two terms.
        🔗
        🔗
      2. In the resulting expression, if both terms have the same binomial factor, then factor this binomial out of both terms.
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Please note that this strategy does not always apply. However, it often does. So much so, that whenever there are four terms, please consider applying the technique of Factoring by Grouping.

    Example 1.6.103.

    Factor \(x^3 + 5x^2 + 2x + 10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Because we are being asked to factor an expression with four terms, we should consider Strategy 1.6.102, the strategy of Factoring by Grouping. We first group the first two terms and the last two terms:
    \begin{equation*} x^3 + 5x^2 + 2x + 10 = (x^3 + 5x^2) + (2x + 10). \end{equation*}
    Next, we factor an expression out of each group:
    \begin{equation*} (x^3 + 5x^2) + (2x + 10) = x^2(x + 5) + 2(x + 5). \end{equation*}
    Now, both terms have the same binomial factor, so we can factor this binomial out:
    \begin{equation*} x^2(x + 5) + 2(x + 5) = (x^2 + 2)(x + 5). \end{equation*}
    Putting all of the work together, we have \(x^3 + 5x^2 + 2x + 10 = x^2(x+5) + 2(x+5) = (x^2+2)(x+5)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    As always, we can check our factoring work by distributing: \((x^2+2)(x+5) = x^3 + 5x^2 + 2x + 10\text{,}\) which we saw in Example 1.6.13.

    Note 1.6.104.

    Before doing another example, let’s discuss several matters that often arise when applying the strategy of Factoring by Grouping.
    • Strictly speaking, there is no need to actually write parentheses around the two groups of terms. That is, from
      \begin{equation*} x^3 + 5x^2 + 2x + 10 \end{equation*}
      we can skip writing
      \begin{equation*} (x^3 + 5x^2) + (2x + 10) \end{equation*}
      and go directly to
      \begin{equation*} x^2(x + 5) + 2(x + 5) \end{equation*}
      instead.
      🔗
      When going from
      \begin{equation*} x^3 + 5x^2 + 2x + 10 \end{equation*}
      directly to
      \begin{equation*} x^2(x + 5) + 2(x + 5), \end{equation*}
      we are essentially factoring by grouping without explicitly writing parentheses to draw the groups. Often, an additional question arises when not drawing in the parentheses: are we allowed to selectively factor like this, where we factor the first two terms together and the last two terms together? The answer is yes, and the most convincing way to see this is to think about what happens backwards. If we were given
      \begin{equation*} x^2(x + 5) + 2(x + 5) \end{equation*}
      and asked to write this expression differently, we’d distribute to get
      \begin{equation*} x^3 + 5x^2 + 2x + 10. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    • Once we have
      \begin{equation*} x^2(x + 5) + 2(x + 5), \end{equation*}
      it’s important to not stop here. Yes, this step is good, but we should factor completely. (In fact, whenever we see the instructions to a math problem is to “factor”, we should factor completely. This means that we keep factoring until we can factor no more.)
      🔗
      🔗
    • One extremely common confusion is how we got from \(x^2(x+5) + 2(x+5)\) to \((x^2 + 2)(x+5)\text{.}\) Why didn’t we get \((x^2+2)(x+5)^2\) instead? There are four different ways that I wish to address this. I hope that you will earnestly read through all four responses:
      • First, let us note that in a different problem, if we had \(y^2u + 2u\) and we were asked to factor out \(u\text{,}\) we’d write either \(u(y^2+2)\) or \((y^2+2)u\text{,}\) but we wouldn’t write \((y^2+2)u^2\text{.}\) The thing that gets factored out doesn’t get an exponent of \(2\) attached to it, whether in this example or in our more complicated example. In fact, the formula we are referencing here is \((b+c)a=bc+ca\text{,}\) but note that the left side is \((b+c)a\) and not \((b+c)a^2\text{.}\)
        🔗
        So, how does this comment apply to our example? In \(x^2(x+5) + 2(x+5)\text{,}\) the expression we are factoring out is \((x+5)\text{,}\) and this expression is admittedly more complicated than factoring out a single variable like \(u\text{,}\) but it is still true that the thing that gets factored out doesn’t get an exponent of \(2\) attached to it.
        🔗
        If you’ll continue with me for a second, let’s take \(x^2(x+5) + 2(x+5)\) and temporarily replace each \((x+5)\) with \(u\text{,}\) as a bookkeeping device. Then, we have \(x^2u + 2u\text{,}\) and factoring out \(u\) gives \(u(x^2 + 2)\) or \((x^2+2)u\text{.}\) Now, we said that the replacement was temporary. Since we replaced \((x+5)\) with \(u\text{,}\) we now replace \(u\) with \((x+5)\) in \((x^2+2)u\) to get \((x^2+2)(x+5)\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      • As a second explanation, note that we had already checked our factoring by distributing: \((x^2+2)(x+5) = x^3 + 5x^2 + 2x + 10\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      • As a third explanation, what would happen if we took the expression that I’m claiming is incorrect, namely \((x^2+2)(x+5)^2\text{,}\) and distributed? We’d get
        \begin{align*} (x^2+2)(x+5)^2 &= (x^2+2)(x^2 + 10x + 25)\\ &= x^4 + 10x^3 + 25x^2 + 2x^2 + 20x + 50\\ &= x^4 + 10x^3 + 27x^2 + 20x + 50 \end{align*}
        which is not equal to \(x^3 + 5x^2 + 2x + 10\text{,}\) the expression we started with.
        🔗
        🔗
      • As a fourth explanation, we can represent \((x^2+2)(x+5)\) geometrically: The big rectangle has area \(x^3 + 5x^2 + 2x + 10\text{,}\) by summing the areas of the four non-overlapping small rectangles, which matches the original expression we started with. For a geometric interpretation of what factoring by grouping does, it is putting the four small rectangles into two groups (in this diagram, one group is the two top rectangles, and the other group is the two bottom rectangles).

    Warning 1.6.106.

    We mentioned that certain parentheses can be thrown in optionally, just to visually keep the two groups together. When we do this, we should be careful about exactly where the parentheses go. Using our previous question to illustrate, we can turn \(x^3 + 5x^2 + 2x + 10\) into
    \begin{equation*} (x^3 + 5x^2) + (2x + 10)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    but we should not write
    \begin{equation*} (x^3 + 5x^2) (+ 2x + 10). \end{equation*}
    The difference here is so slight (the second left parenthesis was placed before the plus sign), but this changes the meaning. What’s the issue here? In writing
    \begin{equation*} (x^3 + 5x^2) (+ 2x + 10) \end{equation*}
    even if there were extra spaces between the two factors:
    \begin{equation*} (x^3 + 5x^2)\,\,(+ 2x + 10). \end{equation*}
    a missing operation symbol is a hidden multiplication sign.

    Example 1.6.111.

    Factor \(p^3-3p^2-4p+12\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(p^3-3p^2-4p+12 = (p^3-3p^2)-(4p-12) = p^2(p-3)-4(p-3) = (p^2-4)(p-3)\) However, our work is not complete, because we can factor \(p^2-4\) further: \((p^2-4)(p-3) = (p+2)(p-2)(p-3)\text{.}\) Putting all our work together, we have \(p^3-3p^2-4p+12 = p^2(p-3)-4(p-3)= (p^2-4)(p-3) = (p+2)(p-2)(p-3)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The last example shows an important point: after applying the strategy of Factoring by Grouping, we should always check to see if the factors we obtained can be factored further. It can also occur that we make our work easier if we factor out a greatets common factor from all four terms first, and then apply Factoring by Grouping.
    🔗

    Example 1.6.112.

    Factor \(4m^8 - 8m^7 - 60m^5 + 120m^4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    All four terms have a common factor of \(4m^4\text{,}\) so we factor this out first:
    \begin{equation*} 4m^8 - 8m^7 - 60m^5 + 120m^4 = 4m^4(m^4 - 2m^3 - 15m + 30). \end{equation*}
    Next, we apply Factoring by Grouping to the expression in parentheses, so
    \begin{equation*} 4m^4(m^4 - 2m^3 - 15m + 30) = 4m^4((m^4 - 2m^3) - (15m - 30)) = 4m^4(m^3(m - 2) - 15(m - 2)) = 4m^4(m^3 - 15)(m - 2). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    As part of the process of factoring completely, it is always helpful to see if there is a greatest common factor of all terms first, whether or not Factoring by Grouping follows next. This is because factoring out a greatest common factor first can often make the remaining expression easier to factor, because the pieces that we’ll see will involve smaller numbers.
    🔗

    Example 1.6.113.

    Factor \(x^7 + 10x^6 + 24x^5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Because there’s three terms instead of four, we won’t be using Factoring by Grouping for this question. However, all three terms have a factor of \(x^5\text{,}\) so we factor this out first:
    \begin{equation*} x^7 + 10x^6 + 24x^5 = x^5(x^2 + 10x + 24). \end{equation*}
    Next, we factor the quadratic expression in parentheses:
    \begin{equation*} x^5(x^2 + 10x + 24) = x^5(x+4)(x+6). \end{equation*}
    Putting all our work together, we have \(x^7 + 10x^6 + 24x^5 = x^5(x^2 + 10x + 24) = x^5(x+4)(x+6)\text{.}\)

    Subsection Factoring quadratics revisited

    So far when we’ve factored quadratics \(Ax^2 + Bx + C\text{,}\) we have focused on situations when the quadratic coefficient is \(A=1\) as in \(x^2 + 8x+15\) or \(x^2 - 5x - 14\text{.}\) Now we look at situations where \(A \ne 1\text{,}\) such as \(4x^2 + 11x + 6\text{.}\) We have to slightly modify two of our existing techniques. These are the more general versions of two techniques we saw earlier.
    🔗
    First, we generalize the method of factoring quadratics from Strategy 1.6.49 that works regardless of the value of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Strategy 1.6.114.

    To factor \(Ax^2+Bx+C\text{,}\) draw a rectangle and divide it into four smaller rectangles. Label the top left rectangle with \(Ax^2\) and the bottom right rectangle with \(C\text{.}\) In the preliminary version of this method, we labeled two side lengths with \(x\text{,}\) but we don’t do that here. Consider the expression \((A \cdot C) \cdot x^2\text{,}\) which can also be written \(ACx^2\text{.}\) We need to find two expressions that multiply to \(ACx^2\) and add to \(Bx\text{.}\) To do this, it’s helpful to first focus on \(ACx^2\text{.}\) Write a list of pairs of factors that multiply to \(ACx^2\) with each factor containing an \(x\text{.}\) In writing the list, note if the two factors will have the same sign or different sign based on the sign of \(AC\text{.}\) Optionally, use the sign of \(Bx\) to determine if certain pairs can be left out of the list. Then, from this list, find the pair that adds to \(Bx\text{.}\) Once the two expressions are found, place them in the two empty rectangles, since they will represent areas. To label the side lengths, in each row and column, find the greatest common factor of the two areas in that row or column. Finally, write the factored form using the side lengths, and applying the geometric interpretation of addition (as the gluing together of sticks).
    🔗
    🔗
    We generalize the method of factoring quadratics from Strategy 1.6.47 that works regardless of the value of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Strategy 1.6.116.

    To factor \(Ax^2+Bx+C\text{,}\) draw the following diagram: The remaining two spaces (on the left and right) need to be filled with two numbers that multiply to the number \(AC\) at the top of the diamond, and add to \(B\) at the bottom of the diamond. It is often helpful to write all the factor pairs of \(AC\text{,}\) and then go through the list to see which pair of numbers adds to \(B\text{,}\) using the signs of \(AC\) and \(B\) to help narrow down the possibilities. Once the two numbers are found, multiply each by \(x\text{,}\) and then consider the sum of these together with \(Ax^2\) and \(C\text{.}\) This will consist of four terms, so we can apply Strategy 1.6.102 to factor the expression completely.

    Subsection Summary

    1. When asked to factor, be sure to factor completely.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. To factor quadratics, in the case of a quadratic coefficient of \(A=1\text{,}\) there is a shortcut method that establishes the signs first and allows us to then only think about placing positive numbers, with the signs already in place.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. More generally, the Diamond Method and the Geometric Method for factoring both address quadratics with any quadratic coefficient, including \(A=1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. When asked to factor an expression with four terms, please consider using Factoring by Grouping. Though this technique is not guaranteed to work, it is often the right tool when there are four terms.
